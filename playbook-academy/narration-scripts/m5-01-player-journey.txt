Playbook follows what we call a material escalation model. [pause] It starts with what every player actually wants, which is learning how to play. And it builds toward what long-term regulars eventually need. Most players never reach the later stages. [emphatically] That's by design.

[speaking thoughtfully] There are five stages.

At Awareness, the player hasn't signed up yet. Playbook appears as a companion brand alongside the product. It teaches how the game works. Odds, rules, what the numbers mean. It makes gambling less intimidating. [emphatically] This is not a safety section. It's a product feature.

[pause]

At the New Player stage, once they know the basic rules, you introduce myth-busting. [air quotes] "I'm due for a win." [air quotes] "This machine is hot." [pause] These are cognitive distortions, and Playbook addresses them through education, not warnings.

At the Active Player stage, the player is settled in. This is where entertainment literacy lives. Finer points, strategy where it applies, quizzes, game guides. The player is engaged enough to want more.

[speaking slowly] At the Regular stage, and only at this stage, you introduce self-management tools. Activity tracking, budgeting, limit-setting. The relationship is established, so these feel like a natural upgrade, not a warning sign.

[reassuringly] And Support Seeking can happen at any point. This is Tier 2. Direct path to support resources. No friction, no forms, no questions asked.

[pause]

[emphatically] Notice what Playbook does not do. [pause] It doesn't push deposit limits on Day One. It doesn't surface self-exclusion tools during onboarding. That's the generic responsible gambling approach, and it fails because players don't have a relationship with the brand yet.

[speaking confidently] Instead, Playbook earns the relationship in stages. Education first. Then tools.
