## Section 1: What to Measure

### Reading

{{PROGRAM_NAME}} is a content brand, not a clinical intervention. The metrics reflect that distinction.

**What to measure — player engagement:**

| Metric | What it tells you |
|--------|-------------------|
| Quiz completions | Is the content engaging enough to finish? |
| Content engagement | Time on page, scroll depth, click-through on {{PROGRAM_NAME}} content |
| Tool adoption | Percentage of players with at least one active limit (deposit, session, loss) |
| Feature usage | Session reminder activations, bankroll planner usage |
| Share rate | How often players share quiz results or myth-busters |
| Return visits | How often players come back to the content hub |

**What to measure — content performance:**

| Metric | What it tells you |
|--------|-------------------|
| Quiz completion rate | Is content engaging enough that players finish what they start? |
| Share rate | Is content interesting enough to share voluntarily? |
| Helpline click-through | Are support resources findable when needed? |
| Deposit limit adoption | Are tools framed effectively as features, not restrictions? |
| Social engagement rate | Does content earn attention on social media? |

**What NOT to measure:**

This boundary is deliberate and non-negotiable:
- **"Problem gambling reduction"** — {{PROGRAM_NAME}} doesn't treat conditions
- **"Harm prevention"** — {{PROGRAM_NAME}} prevents nothing; it informs
- **"At-risk player identification"** — {{PROGRAM_NAME}} doesn't diagnose

Measure engagement. Measure adoption. Measure whether players find the content useful and interesting. That's the standard. Clinical outcomes are outside the scope of a content brand, and claiming otherwise misrepresents what the system does.

This isn't just a philosophical point — it's a practical one. If your success metrics include "problem gambling reduction," you're measuring something {{PROGRAM_NAME}} isn't designed to deliver, which will make the program look like it's failing even when it's succeeding. Set the right metrics and you can demonstrate real value.

>

>
> [show "what not to measure"] Here's the boundary. Do not measure "problem gambling reduction." Do not measure "harm prevention." Do not measure "at-risk player identification." {{PROGRAM_NAME}} is a media brand, not a clinical program. Measuring clinical outcomes would misrepresent what the system does — and make the program look like it's failing even when it's working.
>
> [pause] The right metrics demonstrate real value. The wrong metrics set up a program to fail by measuring things it was never designed to deliver.
>
> [transition] How do you know if the material itself is resonating?

## Section 2: Player Testing

### Reading

The Playbook brand system includes a [Player Testing Protocol]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/docs/player-testing-protocol.md) — a set of survey instruments that test whether content resonates with players, what they remember, and whether cultural adaptations improve the experience. It's designed for marketing teams and requires no research expertise.

**Three instruments, escalating in depth:**

| Instrument | Items | Time | When to use |
|-----------|-------|------|-------------|
| **Pulse survey** | 3 items | Under 1 minute | After any content launch, campaign wave, or quiz deployment |
| **Full survey** | 7 items | About 2 minutes | When you need to understand *why* content is or isn't working |
| **Recall survey** | 4 items | Administered 7-14 days after exposure | To measure what stuck |

The **Pulse survey** gives you a quick signal: NPS (would players recommend this content?), a memorable/forgettable rating, and an open-text improvement suggestion. Minimum 30 responses.

The **Full survey** adds diagnostic depth: five semantic differential pairs that measure whether content is engaging, respectful, clear, and culturally resonant. The *Preachy-Respectful* pair is the brand DNA alarm — if it scores below 4.0, the content may be lecturing players.

The **Recall survey** is a follow-up that measures retention: what do players remember a week or two later? Free recall, true/false fact checks, and behavioral follow-through.

**For cultural adaptation testing**, the Full survey runs as an A/B test — default content vs. adapted version, same questions, compare scores. The primary comparison metric is the *Generic-Made for me* pair.

The total effort to run a test: 3-4 hours spread across 2 weeks. No specialized research staff required.

### Narrator Script

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] The Player Testing Protocol gives you a way to measure whether material resonates — not just whether it exists.
>
> [show three instruments] Three instruments. The Pulse survey takes players under a minute — quick signal after any launch. The Full survey takes two minutes — diagnostic depth when you need to understand why something is or isn't working. The Recall survey is a follow-up a week or two later to measure what stuck.
>
> [show effort] Total effort: 3 to 4 hours spread across two weeks. No research expertise required. It's designed for marketing teams.
>
> [pause] The key metric to watch is the Preachy-Respectful pair in the Full survey. If it drops below 4.0, the material may be lecturing players — which is exactly what {{PROGRAM_NAME}} was designed to avoid.
>
> [transition] Last section — who owns all of this?

## Section 3: Governance and Ownership

### Reading

A brand system is only as good as its stewardship. The [Governance chapter]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/brand-book/08-governance.md) defines who owns the brand, how often it's reviewed, and how updates flow.

**Brand ownership by organization size:**

| Organization size | Recommended owner |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Small operator (< 50 employees) | Marketing lead or product manager |
| Mid-size operator (50-500 employees) | Dedicated program manager |
| Large operator (500+ employees) | Team with brand manager, content strategist, and compliance liaison |
| Regulator / non-profit | Communications director |

The brand owner is responsible for: maintaining the guidelines, reviewing new content for consistency, approving adaptations and exceptions, coordinating with legal/compliance on jurisdiction-specific messaging, training new team members, and managing the relationship with the upstream open-source project.

**Review cadence:**

| Review | Cadence | What it covers |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Quarterly regulatory check** | Every 3 months | Mandatory messaging accuracy, helpline numbers, advertising rules, self-exclusion requirements, new jurisdiction needs |
| **Annual brand review** | Once per year | Brand foundation, visual identity, voice and tone, messaging, accessibility, jurisdictions |
| **Continuous triggers** | As they occur | Regulatory changes (within 30 days), helpline changes (immediately), new jurisdictions (before launch) |

**Cross-functional involvement:** While one person or team owns the brand, these functions should be consulted on relevant decisions:

| Function | Consulted on |
|----------|-------------|
| Legal / compliance | Jurisdiction-specific messaging, required disclaimers, advertising rules |
| Product / UX | Digital touchpoint design, feature placement, user flows |
| Customer service | Scripts, training content, front-line messaging |
| Marketing | Campaign messaging, co-branding, advertising |
| Data / analytics | Engagement metrics, content performance, player behavior |

**The stewardship handoff process** ensures continuity when brand ownership transfers. It includes knowledge transfer sessions, access transfer, relationship mapping, a first audit within 30 days, and a first review cycle within 90 days. The CHANGELOG documents the transition.

### Narrator Script

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Last section: who owns this, and how does it stay current?
>
> [show ownership table] The ownership model scales with organization size. A small operator designates a marketing lead. A mid-size operator assigns a dedicated program manager. A large operator builds a team. The key is that someone is explicitly responsible.
>
> [show review cadence] Two scheduled reviews: quarterly for regulatory accuracy, annually for the full brand system. Plus continuous triggers — helpline changes require immediate action, regulatory changes within 30 days.
>
> [show cross-functional table] The brand owner doesn't work alone. Legal, product, customer service, marketing, and analytics are all consulted on decisions that touch their areas.
>
> [pause] The governance model is designed to prevent drift. Without clear ownership and regular review, guidelines erode. The quarterly cadence is especially important for compliance — gambling regulations change frequently, and material that falls behind stops being trustworthy.

### Question 5

**Assesses:** Learning objective 4

**Stem:** A mid-size operator with 200 employees is launching {{PROGRAM_NAME}}. What is the recommended brand ownership model?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| A | The CEO should personally oversee the brand to ensure executive commitment |
| B | A dedicated program manager who coordinates with legal, product, customer service, marketing, and data/analytics |
| C | A rotating committee of department heads who take turns managing the brand quarterly |

## Key Takeaways — Executive Briefing Program

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] That concludes the Executive Briefing. Three modules, thirty minutes. Here's what to take with you.

- **The engagement gap** is the core problem: player education hasn't been treated with the same creative investment as the entertainment product

- **The business case** is threefold: player retention, brand differentiation, and regulatory compliance

- **Measure engagement and adoption**, not clinical outcomes — the right metrics demonstrate real value
- **Governance requires clear ownership** — designate a brand owner, run quarterly regulatory checks, and conduct an annual brand review
