## Section 1: The Full Stigma-Free Guide

### Reading

In the All Employees program, you learned the top 10 swaps. As a frontline staff member, you need the complete set — all 35 terms. They fall into five categories, each with a different reason for avoidance.

**Category 1: Clinical / Pathological Terms (1-6)**

These belong in medical or regulatory contexts — never in player-facing Tier 1 content or conversation.

| # | Avoid | Use instead | Why |
|---|-------|------------|-----|
| 1 | Problem gambler | Player / person | Defines someone by a clinical label |
| 2 | Gambling addiction | Difficulty with gambling | Clinical diagnosis — Tier 2 only |
| 3 | Pathological gambling | Difficulty with gambling | Outdated clinical term, removed from DSM-5 |
| 4 | Gambling disorder | Difficulty with gambling | Clinical diagnosis, appropriate in Tier 2 only |
| 5 | Compulsive gambling | Repeated patterns / difficulty stopping | Implies the person has lost control |
| 6 | Disordered gambling | — | Clinical category, Tier 2 only |

**Category 2: Judgmental Language (7-12)**

Terms that carry implicit moral judgment about how people spend their time and money.

| # | Avoid | Use instead | Why |
|---|-------|------------|-----|
| 7 | Gambler | Player | "Gambler" carries negative connotations |
| 8 | Irresponsible gambling | — (don't use the frame) | Implies moral failure |
| 9 | Reckless betting | — (don't use the frame) | Moral judgment on behavior |
| 10 | Wasting money | Entertainment spending / play costs | Judging how someone spends money is not our job |
| 11 | Losing control | — (describe the specific behavior) | Implies the person is out of control |
| 12 | Bad habits | Play patterns | "Bad" is a judgment; "patterns" are neutral |

**Category 3: Paternalistic Framing (13-18)**

Terms that position you as parent, teacher, or authority figure instead of a peer.

| # | Avoid | Use instead | Why |
|---|-------|------------|-----|
| 13 | Responsible gambling | Specific behaviors (set a budget, know the odds) | Generic, doesn't drive action, implies irresponsibility |
| 14 | Smart play / Play smart | Specific behaviors | Same issue — generic, slightly condescending |
| 15 | You should | You can / Consider / Here's an option | "Should" is prescriptive; {{PROGRAM_NAME}} offers, doesn't instruct |
| 16 | We recommend | Here's what's available | Sounds like a prescription |
| 17 | You need to | You can | "Need to" implies obligation; "can" implies option |
| 18 | For your own good | — (just state the benefit) | Paternalistic |

**Category 4: Industry Jargon (19-26)**

Clear to regulators and researchers, meaningless or off-putting to players.

| # | Avoid | Use instead | Why |
|---|-------|------------|-----|
| 19 | Intervention | Feature / tool | "Intervention" is done *to* you; "feature" is done *for* you |
| 20 | Harm minimization | — (describe the specific benefit) | Public health jargon |
| 21 | Self-exclude (in Tier 1) | Take a break / Pause your account | "Exclude" sounds punitive; "pause" sounds voluntary |
| 22 | Mandatory responsible gambling message | Required information / Here's what you should know | Don't announce that a message is mandatory — just deliver it well |
| 23 | Restrictions | Limits / your limits | "Restrictions" implies external control; "limits" implies ownership |
| 24 | Self-assess | Check in / Review your play | "Self-assess" is clinical; "check in" is casual |
| 25 | Reality check | Session reminder | "Reality check" implies the player has lost touch with reality |
| 26 | Wagering behavior | Play patterns / activity | Clinical abstraction — use everyday language |

**Category 5: Fear-Based Language (27-35)**

Terms designed to scare rather than inform.

| # | Avoid | Use instead | Why |
|---|-------|------------|-----|
| 27 | Harm | — (describe the specific concern or benefit) | Tier 2 only; in Tier 1 focus on what players gain |
| 28 | Danger / Dangerous | — (state the fact instead) | Fear word — let facts speak |
| 29 | Warning / WARNING | Heads up / Here's what you should know | "Warning" triggers avoidance; "heads up" triggers attention |
| 30 | Risk / At-risk | — (describe the specific situation) | Clinical category — don't label players |
| 31 | Consequences | Costs / what to know | "Consequences" implies punishment |
| 32 | Odds are against you | The house has an edge / Here's the house edge | Same fact, different framing — informative vs. threatening |
| 33 | Lose / You will lose | Play costs / The house edge means... | Deficit-focused; reframe as cost of entertainment |
| 34 | Destroy / Ruin | — (don't catastrophize) | Catastrophizing drives avoidance, not engagement |
| 35 | Victim | Person experiencing difficulty | "Victim" removes agency; person-first preserves it |

**The core principle behind all 35 terms:** Put the person before any condition. Describe behaviors, not people. Offer options, not mandates. And lead with what players gain, not what they should fear.

## Section 2: Tier 1 vs Tier 2 Language

### Reading

You learned the two tiers in the All Employees program. Now let's go deeper into how the language changes between them — because as a frontline staff member, you'll sometimes cross the boundary mid-conversation.

**Context-dependent terms:** Some words are appropriate in Tier 2 but wrong in Tier 1. Know the boundary.

| Term | Tier 1 (player-facing, everyday) | Tier 2 (clinical, support, formal) |
|---|---|---|
| Self-exclusion | "Take a break" / "Pause your account" | "Self-exclusion" is acceptable |
| Gambling disorder | Don't use | Acceptable in clinical referral content |
| Problem gambling | Don't use as a label | Acceptable when describing a recognized concept |
| Treatment | "Support" / "Help" | Acceptable in clinical referral content |
| Intervention | "Feature" / "Tool" | Acceptable in regulatory filings |
| At-risk | Don't use as a player label | Acceptable in internal analytics |
| Harm | Don't use | Acceptable in regulatory and public health contexts |
| Addiction | Don't use | Acceptable in Tier 2 with person-first framing |
| Relapse | "Return to play" (if needed) | Acceptable in clinical support |
| Recovery | "Moving forward" / "Next steps" | Acceptable in Tier 2 support |

**The voice principles — do and don't:**

The [Voice and Tone chapter]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/brand-book/04-voice-and-tone.md) defines six principles. Here's how each one translates to dos and don'ts in conversation:

| Principle | Do | Don't |
|---|---|---|
| **Informed, not alarming** | "The house edge on slots ranges from 2% to 15%" | "The odds are stacked against you" |
| **Your choice, your tools** | "Set your deposit limit" | "You should set a deposit limit" |
| **Specific, not sloganeering** | "Know the house edge before you sit down" | "Be a responsible gambler" |
| **Fun is the point** | "Know your game" | "Know the risks" |
| **Real talk** | Short, clear, conversational | Corporate hedging, clinical jargon, qualifiers |
| **Inclusive by default** | "You" and "they," all verticals | Gendered defaults, assumptions about who's playing |

**Tier tone shifts in conversation:** The same topic changes register depending on context:

| Situation | Tier | Your tone | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Player browsing, asks about deposit limits | Tier 1 | Confident, informative | "Great question. You can set a deposit limit in your account settings — daily, weekly, or monthly. Takes about 10 seconds." |
| Player sounds stressed, asks to pause their account | Tier 2 | Warm, direct | "Absolutely. I can help you with that right now. Are you looking for a short break or something longer?" |
| Player just set a limit | Tier 1 (celebratory) | Positive, light | "You're all set. That's you playing on your own terms." |
| Player mentions they can't afford rent | Tier 2 | Warm, direct, no tools | "I hear you. Free, confidential support is available right now — {{HELPLINE_NUMBER}}, 24/7." |

**The key rule:** If a player uses clinical language — "I think I have a gambling addiction" — don't correct them. Meet them where they are. Respond with empathy and offer the helpline. Their language choice tells you they're in Tier 2 territory.

## Section 3: Spotting and Fixing Problems

### Reading

The stigma-free language guide includes an **8-point audit** for reviewing any piece of content or communication. As frontline staff, you won't be auditing marketing copy — but you will be reviewing your own scripts, emails, and spoken interactions. Here's the checklist, simplified for conversation:

1. **Person-first check**: Am I defining anyone by a condition? ("Problem gambler" defines. "Player" doesn't.)
2. **Label scan**: Did I use any of the clinical terms from the guide? (Search for: problem gambler, addict, compulsive, pathological, at-risk, victim.)
3. **Judgment check**: Am I implying moral failure? (Irresponsible, reckless, wasting money.)
4. **Fear check**: Am I using scare tactics? (WARNING, danger, destroy, ruin.)
5. **Jargon scan**: Am I using industry terms the player won't understand? (Intervention, harm minimization, wagering behavior.)
6. **Paternalism check**: Am I telling the player what they *should* do? (You should, we recommend, for your own good.)
7. **Agency check**: Does the player have choice? ("Your account has been restricted" removes agency. "You've hit your limit" preserves it.)
8. **Read-aloud test**: Does it sound like a friend talking? Or a doctor, a lawyer, or a parent?

**The fastest version:** After any communication, search for these five words: *should, warning, gambler, problem, risk*. If any of them appear, pause and check whether a swap is needed. This catches the most common issues in under 10 seconds.

### Narrator Script

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] You've got the 35 swaps and you can spot issues in examples. But how do you check your own work in real time?
>
> [show 8-point checklist] There's an 8-point audit in the stigma-free language guide. For frontline staff, I'm going to give you the speed version.
>
> [show 5-word scan] After any communication — written or spoken — scan for five words: should, warning, gambler, problem, risk. If any of them show up, check whether a swap is needed. This catches the most common issues in about ten seconds.
>
> [pause] The say-it-out-loud test is the final check. Take what you wrote — or replay what you said — and ask: does this sound like a friend explaining something? Or does it sound like a doctor, a lawyer, or a parent? If it's the latter, revise.
>

### Question 5

**Assesses:** Learning objective 4

**Stem:** Why does {{PROGRAM_NAME}} avoid the term "problem gambler" in Tier 1 content?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| A | Because it's grammatically incorrect |
| B | Because regulators have banned the term in all jurisdictions |
| C | Because it defines a person by a clinical label, which causes people who don't identify with it to disengage and people who do identify with it to feel shame |
| D | Because "player" sounds more professional in a business context |

**Correct:** C

**Explanation:** Labels shut people down in two directions. A player who reads "problem gambler" and doesn't self-identify will dismiss the entire message. A player who does self-identify may feel shame — and shame drives avoidance, not engagement. "Player" is neutral and inclusive, keeping the door open for everyone.

**Source:** [Stigma-Free Language Guide — The Person-First Principle]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/messaging/stigma-free-language.md)

## Key Takeaways

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Here's what to remember.

- **Before writing any player-facing text, open the 35-term guide and scan your draft against it** — check each of the five categories: clinical, judgmental, paternalistic, jargon, and fear-based.
- **Replace every label with a person-first alternative in your next shift**: say "player" not "gambler," describe behaviors not people, offer "you can" not "you should."
- **Ask yourself "which tier?" before every player response**: if the player is browsing or curious, use Tier 1 (confident, informative). If they mention financial stress or use clinical language, switch to Tier 2 (warm, direct, helpline).
- **When a player says "I think I have a gambling addiction," respond with empathy and the helpline** — never correct their word choice or redirect to tools. Their language tells you they need Tier 2.
- **After every email, script, or chat message, search for five words: *should, warning, gambler, problem, risk*** — if any appear, rewrite that sentence before hitting send.
- **Read your message aloud before sending**: if it sounds like a friend explaining something, send it. If it sounds like a doctor, lawyer, or parent, rewrite it until it doesn't.
