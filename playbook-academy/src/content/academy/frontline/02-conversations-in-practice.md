# Conversations in Practice

You know the tools. You know the language. This module puts them together in real conversations. You'll walk through three scenarios that frontline staff encounter regularly — deposit limits, odds questions, and account pauses — and learn the tone principles that make these conversations work. You'll also learn when a conversation has moved beyond your scope and how to hand off without making the player feel dismissed.

## Section 1: Tone Principles for Conversations

### Reading

Every player conversation follows five tone principles. These come from the [Voice and Tone chapter]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/brand-book/04-voice-and-tone.md) and the [Conversation Scripts]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/collateral/customer-service/conversation-scripts.md) general guidelines:

**1. Be warm, not clinical.** You're a person helping another person — not a system delivering a protocol. Use the player's name if you have it. Speak in natural language.

**2. Be direct, not evasive.** If you can help, say how. If you need to transfer, say why. Players can tell when you're stalling or reading from a script they can't see.

**3. Use {{PROGRAM_NAME}} language.** The 35 swaps from Module 01 apply here. Say "tools" not "interventions." Say "pause" not "ban." Say "player" not "gambler." This isn't optional.

**4. Never diagnose.** You are not a counselor. You provide information, tools, and referrals. You never say "It sounds like you have a problem." You never ask "Are you having a problem?" You offer what's available and let the player decide.

**5. Listen first.** Let the player finish before responding. Don't interrupt with scripted language. The fastest way to lose someone's trust is to answer a question they didn't ask.

**The universal dos and don'ts:**

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Use the player's name if available | Use clinical terms (pathological, addicted, disordered) |
| Acknowledge their concern before solving | Minimize their concern ("It's not that bad") |
| Offer options, not mandates | Tell them what they "should" do |
| Mention the helpline naturally, not as a warning | Lead with the helpline before understanding the request |
| Speak at the player's pace | Rush through a script |
| Use simple, clear language | Use jargon or corporate language |

## Section 2: Three Scenarios

### Reading

These three scenarios cover the most common low-risk conversations frontline staff handle. Each one includes what the player might say, how to respond, key phrases to use, phrases to avoid, and when the conversation might escalate.

---

### Scenario A: Player Asks About Deposit Limits

**Trigger:** "How do I set a deposit limit?" / "Can I limit my deposits?" / "Where are my limits?"

**Tone:** Confident / Informative

**The conversation flow:**

**Opening:** Start with validation. The player asked a good question — tell them so.

> "Great question. Deposit limits are one of the most useful tools we have. Let me walk you through it."

**Walkthrough:** Give them the path and the mechanics.

> "You can set a daily, weekly, or monthly deposit limit right in your account settings. Go to [navigation path] and you'll see the option to set your number. Once it's set, it works in the background."

**If they ask "What number should I set?":** Don't prescribe. The amount is their decision.

> "That's completely up to you. Think of it as your entertainment budget — the amount you're comfortable spending in that period. There's no right or wrong number."

**If they ask about changes:** Explain the cooling-off asymmetry.

> "Reducing your limit takes effect immediately. If you want to increase it, there's a cooling-off period of {{COOL_OFF_PERIOD}} — that's to make sure the change is something you still want after some time."

**Closing:** Open the door to other tools without pushing.

> "Is there anything else I can help with? And just so you know — session reminders, your activity dashboard, and other tools are all in the same place."

**Key phrases:** "Your tools, your call" / "Entertainment budget" / "Works in the background" / "Completely up to you"

**Never say:** "You should set a limit" / "This will help you control your gambling" / "Are you having a problem?"

---

### Scenario B: Player Asks How Odds Work

**Trigger:** "How do odds work?" / "What's the house edge?" / "Am I likely to win?"

**Tone:** Confident / Informative

**The conversation flow:**

**Opening:** Lead with clarity, not caution.

> "Happy to explain. Every game has something called a house edge — it's the mathematical advantage built into the game."

**Explanation:** Use real numbers. Specificity is what makes this useful.

> "For example, blackjack with basic strategy has a house edge of about half a percent. American roulette is about 5.26%. Slots can range from 2% to 15% depending on the machine. What this means: over time, for every $100 you wager on American roulette, the casino keeps about $5.26. That's the cost of the entertainment."

**If they ask "So I can't win?":** This is the most important response to get right.

> "You absolutely can win in any given session — that's what makes it exciting. The house edge is a long-term average, not a prediction for each session. Knowing the edge helps you pick your games and set your budget realistically."

**If they ask about "systems" or "strategies":** Present the math, not a judgment.

> "No betting system changes the house edge — it's built into every game. Strategies like basic blackjack strategy can lower the edge, but they can't eliminate it. The math is the math."

**Closing:** Point them to the content hub for more.

> "We have a Game IQ quiz in the {{PROGRAM_NAME}} section that covers all of this — most people find it pretty eye-opening. Want me to send you the link?"

**Key phrases:** "House edge" (not "odds against you") / "Cost of the entertainment" / "Helps you pick your games and set your budget" / "The math is the math"

**Never say:** "The odds are stacked against you" / "You'll probably lose" / "You should only bet what you can afford to lose"

### Scenario C: Player Wants to Pause Their Account

**Trigger:** "I want to take a break" / "Can I pause my account?" / "I need to stop for a while"

**Tone:** Warm / Direct

**The conversation flow:**

**Opening:** Affirm their decision immediately. Then ask about scope.

> "Absolutely. You can pause your account, and I can help you with that right now. Can I ask — are you looking for a short break, like a few days or weeks, or something longer?"

**If short break (cool-off):** Explain the options and what happens.

> "We have a cool-off option that lets you pause for 24 hours, 7 days, or 30 days. During that time, you won't be able to log in or play. When the period ends, your account becomes active again automatically. Would you like me to set that up?"

**If longer break (self-exclusion):** Explain the formal option clearly.

> "We have a self-exclusion option for longer periods — 6 months, 1 year, 5 years, or permanent. During that time, your account is fully closed. You won't receive any marketing, and you won't be able to reopen the account until the period ends. Would you like me to walk you through the options?"

**If they seem uncertain:** Remove pressure. Let them decide at their pace.

> "There's no pressure to decide right now. You can start with a short cool-off if you want to try it out, or go longer if that feels right. It's your call."

**Closing:** Confirm the action and mention support — naturally, not as a warning.

> "I've set that up for you. If you need anything during this time — or just want to talk — free, confidential support is available 24/7 at {{HELPLINE_NUMBER}}. No judgment, ever."

**Key phrases:** "Pause your account" (not "ban yourself") / "Take a break" (not "self-exclude" in opening) / "It's your call" / "No pressure to decide right now"

**Never say:** "Are you sure?" / "Do you have a gambling problem?" / "You're banning yourself" / "You can't come back"

**Escalation:** If the player expresses emotional distress during this conversation — crying, mentioning financial crisis, expressing despair — the conversation has shifted to Tier 2. Stop the tool walkthrough. Listen. Acknowledge. Offer the helpline. This is when you transition to escalation (see Section 3).

## Section 3: When You Don't Know the Answer

### Reading

You won't have every number memorized. That's fine. What matters is knowing where to find it and how to handle the gap confidently.

**The three things you should always know:**
1. What a house edge is and why it matters (you learned this in All Employees)
2. Where to find specific numbers (the game guides in the {{PROGRAM_NAME}} hub)
3. How to explain any tool in one sentence (you practiced this in Module 00)

**When a player asks something you can't answer immediately:**

Say: "That's a great question. Let me find the exact number for you — give me one moment." Then look it up. This is always better than guessing or deflecting.

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't make up a number ("I think the house edge is about 3%...")
- Don't deflect ("You'd have to check our website for that")
- Don't overexplain why you don't know ("Well, there are so many games and the numbers change depending on...")

**The one-sentence framework:** If you can't find the answer in 30 seconds, say: "I want to make sure I give you the right information. Let me check with [the game guide / my supervisor] and follow up. Can I [call you back / send you a link]?"

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Here's a reality of frontline work: you won't know everything. And that's okay. What matters is how you handle the gap.
>
> [show three things to know] You should always know three things: what a house edge is, where to look up specific numbers, and how to explain each tool in one sentence. Everything else, you can find.
>
> [key point] When you don't know, say so — confidently. "Great question. Let me find the exact number." That's better than guessing. Better than deflecting. Better than overexplaining why you don't know. Players respect honesty. They don't respect bluffing.

## Section 4: When to Escalate

### Reading

Most frontline conversations are low-risk — deposit limits, odds questions, feature walkthroughs. But sometimes a conversation signals that the player needs more than you can provide. Knowing when to escalate — and how to do it without making the player feel dismissed — is a critical skill.

**Escalation signals:**

| Signal | What it looks like | Your response |
|---|---|---|
| **Emotional distress** | Crying, angry outbursts, expressing despair | Stop the current conversation. Listen. Acknowledge: "I hear you." Offer the helpline. |
| **Financial crisis** | "I can't afford rent" / "I've lost everything" / asking to borrow money | Don't offer financial advice. Offer the helpline and offer to pause the account. |
| **Verbal: can't stop** | "I can't stop" / "I don't know what to do" / "I've tried everything" | This is Tier 2. Acknowledge, offer the helpline, offer to pause. Don't promote other tools. |
| **Self-harm mention** | Mentioning self-harm, expressing hopelessness, suicide references | Activate your organization's crisis protocol **immediately**. Stay on the line. Refer to crisis line (988 or local equivalent). |
| **Angry about limits** | Demanding you override their self-set limit, becoming verbally abusive | Be patient and firm: "The cooling-off period protects your decision. It applies to everyone." If abusive, follow standard escalation protocol. |

**How to hand off well:**

The worst thing you can do is make the player feel like they're being passed off because their problem is too much for you. Frame the handoff as connecting them with the **right** resource — not removing them from your queue.

Good handoff: "I want to make sure you get the right support. Free, confidential help is available right now at {{HELPLINE_NUMBER}}. They specialize in exactly this kind of conversation."

Bad handoff: "That's not really my area. Let me transfer you."

**The rule for tools during escalation:** When a player is in distress, don't promote tools. Don't say "Have you tried setting a deposit limit?" A player who is crying doesn't need a feature tour. They need to be heard, acknowledged, and connected with support. The only tool you should offer in a Tier 2 moment is an account pause — and only if the player asks for it or it's clearly what they need.
