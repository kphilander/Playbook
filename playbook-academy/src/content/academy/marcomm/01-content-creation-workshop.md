## Section 1: The Message Library

### Reading

The [core messages library]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/messaging/core-messages.md) contains 74 pre-written messages organized by use case. Every message is tagged with its pillar, tone register, tier, and maximum character count.

**Message categories:**

| Category | Count | Primary use |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| Pillar messages | 16 | Campaign headlines, landing pages, section intros |
| Contextual messages by touchpoint | 36 | In-app screens, notifications, transactional moments |
| Seasonal / event messages | 12 | Campaigns tied to sporting events, awareness weeks |
| Regulatory compliance messages | 10 | Age verification, T&Cs, jurisdiction disclaimers |

**How to use the library:**

1. **Identify the touchpoint.** Where will this message appear? (In-app, social, email, print, push notification)
2. **Check the pillar.** Is this about transparency (Open) or engagement (Social)?
3. **Match the tone register.** Is the player browsing (playful/confident), using a tool (celebratory), or reaching out (warm)?
4. **Find the closest message.** Search by touchpoint context in the library.
5. **Customize.** Replace `{{PLACEHOLDER}}` tokens. Adjust length to fit the channel. Adapt cultural profile if needed.

**Sample messages by pillar:**

*Open pillar:*
- O-1: "The house edge on blackjack is 0.5%. On slots, it's 2-15%. On American roulette, it's 5.26%. Now you know."
- O-3: "No fine print. Just the real numbers behind the games you play."

*Social pillar:*
- S-1: "Share this with the friend who thinks they have a 'system.'"
- S-3: "How well do you really know the odds? There's one way to find out."

*Tools pillar:*
- T-1: "Deposit limits take 10 seconds to set. It's like a seatbelt for your bankroll."
- T-2: "Your tools. Your limits. Your call."

## Section 2: Writing in {{PROGRAM_NAME}} Voice

### Reading

Beyond the message library, you'll often need to write original content. Three content types come up most:

**Taglines.** The tagline system has four levels:
1. **Master tagline:** "Know your game." (used everywhere)
2. **Pillar taglines:** "No fine print" (Open), "Worth sharing" (Social)
3. **Feature taglines:** "Your tools. Your limits. Your call."
4. **Campaign taglines:** Created per campaign, retired when done

Rules: keep them under 40 characters. Lead with the player's benefit. Use active voice. Never use clinical language.

**CTAs (Calls to Action).** Three categories:
- Primary CTAs drive engagement: "Take the quiz," "Test your game IQ," "See the real odds"
- Feature CTAs promote tools: "Set your limits," "Try the bankroll planner," "Check your stats"
- Content CTAs drive deeper reading: "Read the breakdown," "Watch the explainer," "See how it works"

Rules: start with a verb. Keep under 25 characters. Make the benefit obvious. Never use "Click here."

**Myth-busting content.** The HOOK/FACT/STAT format:
- **HOOK:** A provocative statement that challenges the myth ("Your 'lucky machine' has the emotional range of a toaster.")
- **FACT:** The truth, stated simply ("Every spin is generated by a random number generator. The machine has no memory of previous results.")
- **STAT:** A specific number that anchors the fact ("The house edge on slots ranges from 2% to 15%, regardless of which machine you choose.")

### Narrator Script

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] The message library handles a lot. But you'll also write original pieces — taglines, CTAs, and myth-busters. Let's look at how.
>
> [show tagline hierarchy] Taglines have four levels, from the master tagline down to campaign-specific ones. Keep them under 40 characters. Lead with the player's benefit. Active voice. No clinical language.
>
> [show CTA examples] CTAs start with a verb. Under 25 characters. Make the benefit obvious. "Take the quiz" works. "Click here" doesn't.
>
> [show HOOK/FACT/STAT] Myth-busting uses the HOOK/FACT/STAT format. A provocative hook, a simple truth, and a specific number. This is the Social pillar in action — material designed to be shared.
>
> [transition to exercise] Time to write.

## Section 3: Quiz and Interactive Content

### Reading

Quizzes are {{PROGRAM_NAME}}'s most powerful engagement tool. They're Social pillar content — designed to test knowledge, challenge assumptions, and be shared.

**Quiz question anatomy:**

| Component | Purpose | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Stem** | The question — clear, specific, no ambiguity | "What happens to the house edge when you increase your bet size on a slot machine?" |
| **Options (A-D)** | Four choices — one correct, three plausible distractors | A: It decreases / B: It stays the same / C: It increases / D: It depends on the machine |
| **Correct answer** | Clearly identified | B |
| **Explanation** | Why the correct answer is right — teaches, doesn't just judge | "The house edge is a fixed percentage built into the game's math. Changing your bet size changes your potential win/loss per spin, but the edge stays the same." |
| **Source** | Links to the deeper content | [Slots Guide]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/how-to-play/slots.md) |

**Quiz writing rules:**
1. One concept per question — don't test two things at once
2. Distractors should be plausible, not absurd
3. The explanation should teach — the learner should walk away smarter even if they got it wrong
4. Every question should make the player want to share their result
5. Never use "all of the above" or "none of the above"

### Narrator Script

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Quizzes are where the Social pillar really shines. They're the pieces most likely to be shared, most likely to be completed, and most likely to change how someone thinks about a game.
>
> [show question anatomy] Every quiz question has five parts: stem, options, correct answer, explanation, and source. The explanation is the most important part — it's where the actual learning happens.
>
> [show writing rules] Five rules. One concept per question. Plausible distractors. Teaching explanations. Share-worthy results. No "all of the above."
>
> [transition to exercise] Write one yourself.

## Section 4: Cultural Adaptation

### Reading

The {{PROGRAM_NAME}} system uses a five-dimension cultural profile to adapt content across markets. Every piece of content can be tuned along these axes without abandoning the brand's core voice principles:

| Dimension | Base profile | Adaptation range |
|-----------|-------------|------------------|
| **Voice** | Peer | Peer → Mentor → Authority |
| **Framing** | Individual | Individual → Collective |
| **Humor** | Irreverent | Irreverent → Playful → Respectful → None |
| **Directness** | Blunt | Blunt → Direct → Diplomatic → Indirect |
| **Comfort** | Open | Open → Measured → Cautious |

The rule: **adapt the execution, not the architecture.** The HOOK/FACT/STAT format works everywhere. The two-pillar, two-tier system is universal. What changes is how a hook sounds, how directly a fact is stated, and how much humor a stat carries.

**Example — same message across two profiles:**

*Base profile (peer / irreverent / blunt):*
"Your 'lucky machine' has the emotional range of a toaster. Every spin is random. The house edge stays the same no matter which machine you pick."

*Adapted profile (mentor / respectful / diplomatic):*
"Many players believe certain machines are luckier than others. In fact, every spin is determined by a random number generator. The house edge remains constant regardless of which machine you choose."

Both deliver the same fact. Both challenge the myth. The first uses irreverent humor and blunt delivery. The second uses respectful framing and diplomatic directness. Neither violates the core voice principles — they're both specific, informative, and empowering.

### Narrator Script

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Everything you've written so far uses the base cultural profile — peer voice, irreverent humor, blunt directness. But {{PROGRAM_NAME}} serves markets where that register doesn't land.
>
> [show five dimensions] The cultural profile has five dimensions. Voice, framing, humor, directness, and comfort. Each one has a range. The base profile sits at one end — your job is to slide along each axis when the target market requires it.
>
> [show comparison] Same myth-bust, two profiles. The base version jokes about a toaster. The adapted version says "Many players believe" — same fact, different delivery. Both are on-brand. Both follow the voice principles.
>
> [pause] The key: adapt the execution, not the architecture. The pillars, tiers, and message formats stay the same. The cultural dimensions change how you deliver them.
>
> [transition to exercise] Now try it yourself.

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Five questions to check what you've learned. You need 80% — that's 4 out of 5 — to pass. Each question puts you in a real content creation scenario.

### Question 1

**Assesses:** Learning objective 1

**Stem:** You're building an in-app notification to promote the new session reminder feature. You search the 74-message library and find three candidates. Which message is the best fit for a push notification about a time-management tool?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| A | O-1: "The house edge on blackjack is 0.5%. On slots, it's 2-15%. On American roulette, it's 5.26%. Now you know." |
| B | T-1: "Deposit limits take 10 seconds to set. It's like a seatbelt for your bankroll." |
| C | T-3: "Set your budget before you play. It's the one decision that makes every other decision easier." |
| D | S-1: "Share this with the friend who thinks they have a 'system.'" |

**Correct:** C

**Explanation:** T-3 is Tools pillar, warm/direct tone, and focuses on the planning decision — the closest match for promoting a time-management feature like session reminders. T-1 is also Tools pillar but specifically about deposit limits. O-1 is Open pillar (odds education), and S-1 is Social pillar (sharing). Always match the message's pillar and touchpoint to your context.

**Source:** [Core Messages]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/messaging/core-messages.md)

### Question 2

**Assesses:** Learning objective 2

**Stem:** A colleague drafts this campaign tagline: "Be responsible — know when to stop gambling before it's too late." Which {{PROGRAM_NAME}} voice principles does this violate?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| A | Only the character limit — it's too long at 62 characters |
| B | It uses clinical language ("gambling") and paternalistic framing ("be responsible"), but the tone is fine |
| C | It violates at least three principles: it's alarming rather than informed ("before it's too late"), prescriptive rather than empowering ("be responsible"), and generic rather than specific (no concrete behavior or number) |
| D | It's fine for a Tier 2 support context but wrong for Tier 1 |

**Correct:** C

**Explanation:** This tagline fails three voice principles. "Before it's too late" is alarming, not informative. "Be responsible" is prescriptive and generic — it tells the player what to be instead of what to do. And there's no specific number, tool, or behavior. A better version: "The house edge on slots ranges from 2% to 15%. Now you know." — specific, informative, and under 40 characters of meaningful content.

**Source:** [Voice and Tone]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/brand-book/04-voice-and-tone.md)

### Question 3

**Assesses:** Learning objective 3

**Stem:** You're writing a quiz question about roulette for the content hub. Your draft stem reads: "What is the house edge and why is it important for responsible gambling?" A colleague flags it during peer review. What's wrong?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| A | The question tests two concepts at once (what the house edge is AND why it matters), and "responsible gambling" is a stigma-free language violation |
| B | Nothing is wrong — the question is clear and educational |
| C | The question is too easy — it should include harder vocabulary |
| D | Roulette questions should only use multiple-choice with numbers, not open-ended framing |

**Correct:** A

**Explanation:** Two issues. First, quiz writing rule #1: one concept per question — this stem asks "what is it" AND "why does it matter," which are two distinct concepts. Second, "responsible gambling" is swap #13 from the stigma-free language guide — replace it with specific behaviors. A better version: "On an American roulette wheel, what gives the house its 5.26% edge?"

### Question 5

**Assesses:** Learning objective 4

**Stem:** You're adapting Campaign 1 (Slots Myths Week) for a market where the cultural profile specifies "respectful" humor instead of "irreverent" and "diplomatic" directness instead of "blunt." Your myth-bust HOOK reads: "Your 'lucky machine' has the emotional range of a toaster." What should you do?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| A | Keep the hook as-is — the HOOK/FACT/STAT format is universal and shouldn't change |
| B | Remove the hook entirely — humor doesn't work in respectful cultures |
| C | Rewrite the hook to soften the humor while keeping the myth-challenging intent — for example: "Many players believe certain machines run hot or cold. The reality may surprise you." |
| D | Replace the myth-bust format with a plain factual statement to avoid any risk of offense |

**Correct:** C

**Explanation:** Cultural adaptation means adjusting the execution, not abandoning the format. The HOOK/FACT/STAT structure works across cultures — but the tone of the hook changes. "Respectful" humor is gentler: it challenges the myth without mocking the player. "Diplomatic" directness softens the delivery without removing the point. Keeping it unchanged ignores the cultural profile; removing humor or the format entirely undermines the Social pillar's shareability.

## Key Takeaways

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Here's what to remember from this workshop.

- **Start from the library** — 74 pre-written messages cover most touchpoints; customize, don't reinvent
- **Taglines**: under 40 chars, player benefit first, active voice, no clinical language
- **CTAs**: start with a verb, under 25 chars, make the benefit obvious
- **Myth-busting**: HOOK/FACT/STAT format — provocative, truthful, specific
- **Quizzes**: one concept per question, plausible distractors, teaching explanations
- **The rewrite test** is your best friend — take any compliance message and make it human

> **Narrator:** The last module in this program is Campaign Planning & Measurement — where you'll learn to take everything you've practiced and build it into a campaign that reaches the right players through the right channels. See you there.
