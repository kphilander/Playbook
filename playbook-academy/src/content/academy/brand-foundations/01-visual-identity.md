## Section 1: Visual System Principles

### Reading

Most responsible gambling content looks like it was designed by a compliance department. Because it usually was. Playbook takes a different approach: player education content should be **visually indistinguishable in quality** from the operator's commercial content.

> **The visual quality rule:** Playbook content must match the production quality of the operator's commercial marketing. If the Playbook content looks generic, players will treat it like fine print.

### The adaptive identity model

Playbook isn't a rigid visual system that every operator copies identically. It's an **adaptive identity** -- operators keep their own colors and fonts, but the structural elements stay consistent:

| What operators customize | What stays consistent |
|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Color palette (primary, secondary, accent) | Layout patterns and spacing |
| Typography (heading + body fonts) | Information hierarchy |
| Logo and co-branding | Icon system (dual stroke-weight) |
| Photography style | Component structure |

The result: a player who encounters Playbook-based content at one operator will intuitively recognize the content structure at another -- even though they look different. It's recognizable without being identical.

### The default palette

Playbook's default colors are chosen to feel like a lifestyle brand, not a health program:

| Role | Color | Hex | Usage |
|------|-------|-----|-------|
| **Primary** | Deep navy | #1B2838 | Brand identity, headers, backgrounds -- authority and sophistication |
| **Secondary** | Electric teal | #00D4AA | Interactive elements, highlights -- energy and modernity |
| **Accent** | Bold orange | #FF6B35 | CTAs, attention -- action and urgency |

Plus a semantic palette that communicates specific states consistently:

| Color | Hex | Usage |
|-------|-----|-------|
| **Success** | #00C853 | Quiz passed, limit set |
| **Warning** | #FFB300 | Approaching limit |
| **Danger** | #FF3D00 | Tier 2 only -- crisis |
| **Info** | #2979FF | Tips, explainers |

> **Semantic colors are functional.** When customizing the palette, keep the semantic colors (success/warning/danger/info) standard. Players learn these associations across products and platforms. Your brand colors express identity; semantic colors express function. Mixing the two creates confusion -- a red CTA button next to a red danger alert loses its signal.

### Typography system

The type system uses three font families, each with a specific purpose:

| Role | Font | Weights | Usage |
|------|------|---------|-------|
| **Headings & UI** | Inter | 300-900 | Headlines, section headers, quiz questions, CTAs, logo |
| **Body text** | Source Sans 3 | 300-600 | Paragraph text, descriptions, form labels |
| **Data & helplines** | Source Code Pro | 400-600 | Helpline numbers, odds displays, RTP values, data |

**Why Source Code Pro for helpline numbers?** It disambiguates 0/O and 1/l/I. When someone is trying to reach a helpline, those distinctions can be the difference between getting help and dialing a wrong number. This is a functional choice, not an aesthetic one.

### Iconography: the weight-contrast system

The Playbook icon system mirrors the logo's typographic contrast -- **Play** (800 weight) vs **BOOK** (300 weight) -- through dual stroke weights:

- **Primary strokes (2px)** -- outer containers, main shapes, defining structure
- **Detail strokes (1px)** -- inner details, accents, secondary elements

This 2:1 ratio creates visual hierarchy within each icon and connects the icon system to the wordmark. The icon library covers 31 icons across 6 categories: game types, odds & math, player tools, content & education, social & sharing, and support & safety.

### Photography direction

Playbook photography should feel like a lifestyle editorial -- candid, editorial-quality shots with cinematic lighting and an energetic mood. The key question: *"Does this image make gambling look like fun entertainment among friends, or does it make it look like an isolated, high-stakes activity?"*

**Do:**
- Cinematic lighting, warm tone, directional light, real people
- Close-up detail, player perspective, natural context
- Real interface, player's POV, action-oriented

**Don't:**
- Dim, moody lighting that feels isolating or exclusionary
- Generic stock filler -- no people, no story, no context

### Text overlay rules

When placing text over photography, Playbook uses gradient overlays to ensure WCAG AA contrast compliance. Never place white text directly on unprocessed photography.

- **Navy gradient** -- primary overlay for most text treatments
- **Teal gradient** -- positive, action-oriented messaging
- **Navy + orange accent** -- myth-busting content

### Narrator Script

> **Narrator:** Playbook's visual system uses three core colors. Navy, the primary brand color, conveys trust and authority. Teal is the accent for interactive elements, links, and calls to action. Bold orange brings energy to Tier 1 material, but disappears completely in Tier 2. The visual system is designed for maximum contrast and accessibility. Every color combination must meet WCAG double-A standards. The typography uses a heading font for hierarchy and a body font for readability. Icons follow a consistent line-weight style. The system is deliberately restrained. Visual noise is the enemy of player education.

## Section 2: Tier 1 vs Tier 2 Visual Rules

### Reading

The two tiers don't just differ in content -- they differ in visual treatment. Both use the same design system, but with very different levels of restraint.

### Tier 1: Entertainment literacy

Tier 1 content is the majority (~95%) of Playbook output. It should be visually engaging, dynamic, and brand-forward -- full color palette, rich layouts, photography, animation, and personality. Notice the shared DNA: navy backgrounds, teal accents, Inter headings, the PlayBOOK wordmark, orange for attention. Tier 1 templates span social cards, story formats, posters, emails, venue signage, rack cards, and digital displays -- each designed to compete with the operator's best commercial content.

### Tier 2: Support and crisis

Tier 2 is where visual restraint becomes critical. The design goal is **zero friction** -- nothing competes with the support information. The contrast is deliberate: strip back to essentials, maximum contrast, no decorative elements, minimal color, large touch targets, and **always light mode** -- dark mode can feel isolating in crisis contexts.

### Visual elements comparison

| Element | Tier 1 treatment | Tier 2 treatment |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Color** | Full palette, rich combinations | Neutral + teal only, maximum contrast |
| **Typography** | Full type scale, varied weights | Large, clear, minimal hierarchy |
| **Layout** | Dynamic grids, cards, visual variety | Single column, generous spacing |
| **Imagery** | Photography, illustration, data viz | None. Text and links only. |

### Interaction and mode comparison

| Element | Tier 1 treatment | Tier 2 treatment |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Interaction** | Quizzes, tools, animations | Simple buttons. No steps. No friction. |
| **Tone** | Playful, confident, witty | Warm, direct, serious. Zero cleverness. |
| **Dark mode** | Follows system preference | Always light mode |

> **Both tiers use the same brand quality.** The difference is restraint, not quality. Tier 2 isn't "uglier Tier 1" -- it's deliberately sparse because visual noise is friction when someone needs help.

### The visual integration test

Before shipping any Playbook content, apply this four-point test. If it passes all four, ship it.

1. **Does it belong?** Place it next to the operator's commercial content. Does it look like it belongs, or does it look bolted on from a different brand?
2. **Would a CMO sign off?** If it looks like a compliance department made it, redesign. The bar is marketing-quality production, not regulatory checkbox.
3. **Does it feel modern?** If it could have been designed in 2010, it's not good enough. Players consume cutting-edge digital content daily -- your education content competes for the same attention.
4. **Is it accessible?** Run the contrast ratios. Check the touch targets. Test with a screen reader. Accessibility is a prerequisite, not an afterthought.

### Narrator Script

> **Narrator:** Tier 1 and Tier 2 use the same brand quality. The difference is restraint, not quality. Tier 1 gets the full visual system. Navy backgrounds, teal accents, bold orange highlights, playful iconography, and branded illustrations. This is where the personality lives. Tier 2 strips everything back. Orange disappears. Decorative elements disappear. The palette reduces to neutrals plus teal for essential actions. Layout goes single-column, maximum six-forty pixels wide. White space increases dramatically. And critically, Tier 2 always renders in light mode, even if the user has dark mode enabled. When someone needs help, every unnecessary visual element is friction.

## Section 3: Accessibility Requirements

### Reading

Accessibility isn't a nice-to-have. If a player can't access your content because of a disability, a device limitation, or a situational constraint (bright sunlight, noisy venue), your education content fails them the same way buried compliance text does. Different barrier, same result: the player doesn't get the information they need.

### WCAG 2.1 AA: the recommended baseline

Every Playbook touchpoint should meet WCAG 2.1 AA. Here's what that means in practice:

**Visual requirements**

| Requirement | Standard | What it means |
|-------------|----------|---------------|
| **Color contrast (text)** | 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text | All foreground/background text combinations must be measurably readable |
| **Color contrast (UI)** | 3:1 for components and graphical objects | Buttons, icons, charts, and controls must be distinguishable from their backgrounds |
| **Touch targets** | Minimum 44x44px | All interactive elements -- buttons, links, form fields -- must be easily tappable |

**Structural and interaction requirements**

| Requirement | Standard | What it means |
|-------------|----------|---------------|
| **Keyboard navigation** | All interactive content must be keyboard-accessible | Tab order makes sense. Focus states are visible. No keyboard traps. |
| **Screen reader support** | Semantic HTML, ARIA labels where needed | Content structure is clear to assistive technology |
| **Text sizing** | Minimum 16px (1rem) body text | No exceptions. Content people should read must be readable. |

**Contrast ratio in practice:**
- White on Navy: 13.5:1 (passes)
- Teal on Navy: 7.2:1 (passes)
- Navy on White: 13.5:1 (passes)
- Light on Light: 1.4:1 (fails)

### Dark mode accessibility

Dark mode introduces specific accessibility challenges. Key rules:

- **Muted text on dark backgrounds** -- neutral_500 (#6B6B8A) on neutral_900 (#1A1A2E) only achieves 3.33:1. This passes for large text but fails for normal text. Use muted text sparingly in dark mode and only at 18px+ or 14px+ bold.
- **Status colors** -- always use dark text on chromatic status backgrounds (success, warning, danger, info), in both light and dark mode. Never use white text on these colors.
- **Images** -- reduce brightness to 85-90% to prevent glare on dark backgrounds.
- **Tier 2 pages always render in light mode** -- dark mode can feel isolating in crisis contexts.

### The Playbook accessibility checklist

Run through this before any content ships:

**Visual & Contrast:**
- Contrast ratios verified -- every text/background combination meets 4.5:1 (normal) or 3:1 (large)
- Touch targets measured -- all interactive elements are 44x44px minimum
- No color-only information -- meaning is conveyed by text, icons, or patterns alongside color
- Helpline numbers in Source Code Pro -- 0/O and 1/l/I character disambiguation verified

**Interaction & Devices:**
- Keyboard tested -- every action can be completed without a mouse
- Screen reader tested -- content makes sense when read linearly
- Alt text on all images -- descriptive for informational images, empty for decorative
- Responsive tested -- works on mobile, tablet, desktop, and assistive devices

### Narrator Script

> **Narrator:** Accessibility isn't optional. It's the foundation of inclusive player education. Every layout must support keyboard navigation and screen readers. Color contrast ratios must meet WCAG double-A minimum. Four-point-five to one for body text, three to one for large text. Never use color alone to convey meaning. All images need descriptive alt text. Touch targets on mobile must be at least forty-four pixels. Interactive elements need visible focus indicators. And Tier 2 pages always render in light mode, because dark mode can feel isolating in crisis contexts. The goal is simple. Every player, regardless of ability, can access every piece of material.

> **Narrator:** LuckyDraw Casino redesigned their responsible gambling page using the tier-based visual system. Before: cluttered layout, gray text on white with poor contrast, decorative gambling imagery mixed with support information, helpline buried at the bottom. And a dedicated Tier 2 support section with stripped-back design. No orange, no decoration, maximum restraint. Accessibility score: ninety-four out of one hundred. The information was reorganized, not rewritten. Visual design did the heavy lifting.

## Key Takeaways

- **The visual quality rule**: Playbook content must match the production quality of the operator's commercial marketing. If it looks like compliance made it, players will treat it like fine print.
- **Adaptive identity**: operators customize colors and fonts, but structural elements (layout, hierarchy, icons, spacing) stay consistent across all implementations.
- **Three core colors**: deep navy (primary), electric teal (secondary), bold orange (accent -- Tier 1 only). Plus semantic colors for functional states.
- **Tier 1 gets the full visual system**. Tier 2 strips everything back -- no orange, no decoration, single column, light mode only. Same quality, maximum restraint.
- **The four-point test**: does it belong, would a CMO sign off, does it feel modern, is it accessible? Pass all four to ship.
- **WCAG 2.1 AA is non-negotiable**: 4.5:1 contrast for normal text, 44x44px touch targets, keyboard navigation, screen reader support. Accessibility is especially critical for Tier 2 -- stressful situations mean higher cognitive load and lower patience.
