## Section 1: The Player Journey

### Reading

Playbook's content follows a **content escalation** model. It starts with what every player wants -- how to play -- and builds toward what long-term regulars need. Most players never reach the later stages, and that's by design.

**The 5 stages:**

| Stage | What's happening | Playbook's role |
|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| **1. Awareness** | Player hasn't signed up yet. Encountering the brand through ads, social, search. | Playbook appears as a **companion brand** alongside the product. Content teaches how the game works -- odds, rules, what the numbers mean. Makes gambling less intimidating. Not a safety section; a product feature. |
| **2. New Player** | Signed up, first sessions. Learning the basics, placing first bets or spins. | Once they know the basic rules, introduce **myth-busting** content -- cognitive distortions like "I'm due for a win" or "this machine is hot." Builds gambling resilience indirectly through education, not warnings. |
| **3. Active Player** | Settled in. Regular sessions, familiar with the product. | **Entertainment literacy.** Finer points, strategy (where applicable), product-specific knowledge. The player is engaged enough to want to learn more -- quizzes, game guides, content hub articles. |
| **4. Regular** | Long-term player. Predictable pattern of play over weeks or months. | Now introduce **self-management tools** -- activity tracking, budgeting, limit-setting. The relationship is established, so these feel like a natural upgrade, not a warning sign. |
| **5. Support Seeking** | Player or someone close to them is looking for help. | Tier 2 content. Direct path to support resources -- no friction, no forms, no questions asked. |

> This is not a funnel. Most players engage with Stages 1-2 only. Some reach Stage 3. A minority become Regulars. Support Seeking can happen at any point. The framework tells you what content to have ready -- not that every player will need it.

### The content escalation logic

Notice what Playbook does **not** do: it doesn't push deposit limits on Day 1 or surface self-exclusion tools during onboarding. That's the generic RG approach -- and it fails because players don't have a relationship with the brand yet.

Instead, Playbook earns the relationship in stages:

**Stage 1-2: Education first**
"Here's how the game works. Here's what most people get wrong." The player sees Playbook as useful -- a companion that helps them play smarter. No compliance language, no tool-pushing.

**Stage 3-4: Tools earn their moment**
Only after weeks of engagement -- when the player trusts the brand -- do self-management tools appear. They arrive as features, not interventions. "Your activity dashboard is ready" instead of "You should set a limit."

### Key transitions

Three moments where the content strategy shifts:

**New -> Active:** Game education gives way to myth-busting and entertainment literacy. The player has the basics -- now build resilience through cognitive distortion content.

**Active -> Regular:** From education to tools. Activity dashboards, budgeting features, limit-setting -- introduced as upgrades, not interventions. The relationship earns this moment.

**Any -> Support:** Tier 2 content. Direct path to support -- no friction, no marketing. Available at any stage, always one tap away.

> Playbook's content escalation starts with what every player wants -- **how to play** -- and builds toward what regulars need -- **self-management tools**. Most players only see the educational content, and that's fine. The early stages (game education, myth-busting) build resilience on their own. Tools arrive later, when they've been earned.

### Exercise: Journey Stage Check

**Type:** scenario

**Instructions:** For each scenario, identify which journey stage the player is at and choose the most appropriate content to show them. Think about what Playbook's content escalation model says about each stage.

**Scenario 1 of 4**

A player signed up two days ago. They have completed one deposit, played three short blackjack sessions, and just opened the "How does blackjack work?" help article.

- A) Stage 4: Regular -- show them activity dashboards and deposit limit tools
- B) Stage 2: New Player -- show them myth-busting content about the gambler's fallacy
- C) Stage 3: Active Player -- show them advanced strategy content and quizzes
- D) Stage 1: Awareness -- show them a brand introduction ad

**Correct:** B

**Explanation:** This player just signed up and is in their first sessions. They already know the basic rules (they have played a few sessions), so it is time to introduce myth-busting content -- cognitive distortions like "I'm due for a win." Stage 2 builds gambling resilience through education, not warnings. Showing tools this early (Stage 4) would violate the content escalation model.

**Scenario 2 of 4**

A player has been active for four months. They log in three to four times per week, play casino and sportsbook, and recently completed a Game IQ quiz from your content hub. They have never used any self-management tools.

- A) Stage 2: New Player -- they still need basic game education
- B) Stage 5: Support Seeking -- they might need help if they play that often
- C) Stage 3: Active Player -- show them entertainment literacy content, finer strategy points, and more quizzes
- D) Stage 4: Regular -- introduce activity dashboards and budgeting tools

**Correct:** C

**Explanation:** Four months of consistent engagement, multi-product usage, and proactive content consumption (completing quizzes) place this player squarely in Stage 3. They are engaged enough to want deeper knowledge. Entertainment literacy -- finer points, strategy, product-specific content -- is exactly right. They are not yet at the Regular stage where tools get introduced because more engagement time is needed to earn that moment.

**Scenario 3 of 4**

A player has been a steady weekly user for over a year. They play online slots for 30-60 minute sessions, have a consistent deposit pattern, and just received an email about your new activity dashboard feature. They click through.

- A) Stage 3: Active Player -- show them more game guides and quizzes
- B) Stage 4: Regular -- introduce the activity dashboard, budgeting features, and limit-setting as product upgrades
- C) Stage 2: New Player -- show them myth-busting content about slot RNG
- D) Stage 5: Support Seeking -- a year of weekly play means they may need intervention

**Correct:** B

**Explanation:** Over a year of steady, consistent play with no escalation signals is a textbook Regular. This is exactly the moment to introduce self-management tools. The player trusts the brand, so an activity dashboard feels like a feature upgrade, not an intervention. Framing matters: "Your activity dashboard is ready" instead of "You should set a limit."

**Scenario 4 of 4**

Someone visits your site for the first time after seeing a social media ad about a myth-busting quiz. They have not created an account. They click the quiz, answer three questions about slot odds, and browse two related articles.

- A) Stage 2: New Player -- they are engaging with myth-busting content
- B) Stage 4: Regular -- they clearly know about gambling if they are taking quizzes
- C) Stage 1: Awareness -- Playbook appears as a companion brand, teaching how games work through the quiz experience
- D) Stage 5: Support Seeking -- they may be researching gambling problems

**Correct:** C

**Explanation:** This visitor has not signed up -- they are encountering the brand for the first time through an ad. At Stage 1 (Awareness), Playbook appears as a companion brand alongside the product. The quiz and articles serve as game education: how odds work, what the numbers mean. This makes gambling less intimidating and positions Playbook as a useful product feature, not a safety warning. The content is doing its job even though the visitor may never create an account.

## Section 2: Content & Campaigns

### Reading

Playbook ships with a complete campaign framework: pre-built campaigns, a myth-busting library, and a message hierarchy that scales from a tagline to a 500-word article.

**The message hierarchy:**

| Level | What it is | Where it appears |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Level 1: Taglines** | Interchangeable taglines organized by pillar | Ads, hero banners, campaigns |
| **Level 2: Pillar messages** | One core message per pillar | Campaign themes, landing pages |
| **Level 3: Contextual** | Specific to a touchpoint, audience, or action | Deposit screens, email subject lines |
| **Level 4: Long-form** | Educational, explanatory, or interactive | Articles, video scripts, quiz content |

### Ready-to-run campaigns

Playbook ships with pre-built campaigns. The table shows which verticals each supports -- including how casino campaigns adapt for online casino:

|----------|----------|:------------:|:------------:|:----------:|:------:|

| **Beat the House?** | 10 days | Yes | Yes | -- | -- |

| **Luck, Numbers & Lotteries** | 1 week | -- | -- | -- | Yes |
| **The Money Myths** | 10 days | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| **Game IQ Challenge** | 1 week | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |

> **Online casino note:** Both Slots Myths Week and Beat the House? translate directly to online casino -- the myths (RNG, "hot" machines, near-misses, house edge) are identical whether the game is on a physical floor or a screen. The only adaptation needed is swapping physical references ("walk away from the machine") for digital ones ("close the session").

**Scheduling rules:** Avoid peak gambling periods. Allow one week between campaigns. Plan for four campaigns per year. Start with one well-executed campaign rather than trying to launch everything at once.

### Myth-busting as content engine

Myth-busting is Playbook's core content category. The library contains researched myths organized by game type -- each pre-written in multiple formats:

- **Social card** -- 50-80 words. Punchy, shareable. Instagram, X/Twitter, in-app cards.
- **Article explainer** -- 300-500 words. Explains the math, links to tools and quizzes. Content hub, blog, email.
- **Quiz question** -- Four options + instant feedback + "learn more" link. Game IQ quiz, social media.

One myth per week gives you months of content across multiple channels simultaneously.

Each myth in the library is tagged with the underlying cognitive distortion (like the Gambler's Fallacy or the Near-Miss Effect). You can filter by game type or by distortion to find exactly the right piece for your campaign.

> The framework is simple: **phases** tell you where content goes, **segments** tell you how to frame it, **campaigns** give you ready-to-run content packages, and **myth-busting** is your content engine. Start with one campaign for your primary vertical, schedule it for a shoulder period, and build from there.

## Key Takeaways

- **Content escalation model:** Five stages from Awareness to Support Seeking -- education first, tools later
- **Not a funnel:** Most players engage with Stages 1-2 only, and that's by design
- **Education earns the relationship:** Game education and myth-busting come first; self-management tools arrive only after weeks of engagement
- **Support Seeking is always available:** Tier 2 content is accessible at any stage, one tap away
- **Message hierarchy scales:** From taglines (Level 1) to long-form articles (Level 4)
- **Myth-busting is the content engine:** Pre-written in three formats (social card, article, quiz), tagged by cognitive distortion, one per week gives months of content
- **Scheduling matters:** Avoid peak periods, one week between campaigns, four per year, start with one
