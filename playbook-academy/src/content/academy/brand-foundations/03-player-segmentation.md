## Section 1: A New Segmentation Model

### Reading

Traditional gambling industry segmentation groups players by demographics (age, gender) or product (sports bettors, casino players, lottery buyers). This creates segments that describe *what* people do, but not *how they relate to gambling* -- which is what actually determines what content they need.

A 22-year-old and a 55-year-old who both play recreationally and understand the odds need the same content. But a "sports bettor" who just placed their first parlay and one who tracks vig across 15 sportsbooks need completely different content -- even though both are "sports bettors." Demographics and product choice don't tell you what someone needs -- their relationship to gambling does.

Playbook uses a **relationship-based segmentation model**. Six segments, defined by the player's knowledge, engagement pattern, and behavioral indicators.

**The three engagement-focused segments:**

| Segment | Relationship | What they need | Tone |
|---------|-------------|---------------|------|
| **New / Novice** | Low knowledge of a specific product. Recently started or encountering a product for the first time. | How the product works, what the numbers mean, where risks exist. Foundational education. | Confident / Informative |
| **Recreational** | Plays within means, moderate-to-good knowledge, steady patterns. The majority of players. | Engagement, not education. Myth-busting, social content, tools framed as features. | Playful / Witty |
| **Enthusiast** | High engagement, deep product knowledge, tracks their own play. Not problematic -- just highly engaged. | Advanced content, new perspectives, community. Respect their expertise. | Playful / Witty (peer level) |

**The three support-focused segments:**

| Segment | Relationship | What they need | Tone |
|---------|-------------|---------------|------|
| **At-Risk** | Behavioral indicators suggest players in difficulty. Escalating patterns. | Non-judgmental tool visibility, check-in moments, accessible support. | Warm / Direct |
| **In-Crisis** | Actively seeking help or showing severe distress. Tier 2 territory. | Immediate, frictionless access to support. Zero barriers. | Warm / Direct (serious) |
| **Friends & Family** | Not a player. Concerned about someone else's gambling. | Separate entry point. Conversation starters, support resources, validation. | Warm / Direct |

**What makes this model different:**

Three things distinguish this from traditional approaches:

1. **It's relational, not demographic.** A 22-year-old and a 55-year-old who both play recreationally need the same content. Age doesn't determine what someone needs -- their relationship to gambling does.
2. **It's product-contextual.** A player can be Recreational in casino and New/Novice in sports simultaneously. Segment is tied to a specific product, not to the person as a whole.
3. **It's dynamic.** Players move between segments as their behavior changes. Segments describe where a player is *right now*, not who they are permanently.

> Most Playbook content works for all segments without modification. The general voice is your default. Segment-specific adaptation is for targeted campaigns, segment-specific touchpoints, or when behavioral data identifies a match.

## Section 2: Product Vertical Overlay

### Reading

Segments describe *who* the player is in relationship to gambling. Product verticals describe *what* they're playing. These are independent dimensions that overlay each other -- think of it as a grid.

Playbook covers five verticals: **sports betting**, **casino**, **lottery**, **poker**, and **bingo**. Each has its own knowledge domains, its own misconceptions, and its own tool categories.

A Recreational casino player needs session management tools and myth-busting about hot and cold machines. A Recreational sports bettor needs bankroll planning and education about vig and parlay margins. Same segment, completely different content.

**Why the overlay matters:**

Without the overlay, you end up with either:

- **Generic content** -- "Know the odds!" -- that's too vague for anyone to act on. *Which* odds? Parlay odds? Slot RTP? Lottery jackpot probability? Each requires completely different explanations.
- **Product-only segments** -- "Sports Bettors" as a segment -- that ignores that a first-time sports bettor and a 5-year veteran need totally different things, even though they use the same product.

The overlay gives you specificity. A **New/Novice + Sports** player needs to learn what a -110 line means. A **Recreational + Sports** player already knows that -- they need bankroll planning for their NFL season. Same product, different relationships, different content.

> The segment-vertical overlay gives you specificity that neither dimension provides alone. A player's relationship to gambling tells you how to communicate; the product vertical tells you what to communicate about.

## Section 3: Identifying Segments in Practice

### Reading

You don't need perfect data to segment your players. Playbook uses three levels of identification that work at any level of data sophistication.

**Level 1: Structural identification (no data needed)**

Your product already tells you who's where. You just need to read the signals:

| Signal | What it tells you | Segment + Vertical |
|--------|------------------|-------------------|
| Player is in onboarding / first session | They're new to your product | **New/Novice** + whatever vertical they're using |
| Player is browsing the sportsbook | They're engaging with sports betting | Sports vertical (segment depends on other signals) |
| Player is on the support / helpline page | They may be seeking help | **In-Crisis** (or information-seeking -- context matters) |
| Visitor arrived via "worried about someone" link | They're a concerned other, not a player | **Friends & Family** |
| Player is browsing "How to play" content | They're learning the product | **New/Novice** for that product |

> Structural identification isn't a compromise -- it's often more accurate than behavioral models because it's based on what players are actually doing, not what a model predicts they'll do.

**Level 2: Basic data (registration + activity)**

With basic data -- account age, deposit frequency, session counts, product usage -- you can infer more:

- **Account age + consistent activity -> Recreational.** Someone who's been playing steadily for months with no escalation is likely Recreational.
- **Multi-vertical usage + high frequency -> potential Enthusiast.** Regular play across multiple products, especially with proactive tool use, suggests deep engagement.
- **First deposit + first session -> New/Novice.** The most reliable signal. If they just signed up, they're new to your platform regardless of their experience elsewhere.
- **Referral from support search -> Friends & Family.** A visitor arriving via "gambling help for families" is almost certainly a concerned other.

**Level 3: Behavioral analytics**

With full behavioral data -- play patterns, session trends, limit-setting history, content engagement -- you unlock the most important capability: **At-Risk detection**.

Behavioral indicators for At-Risk:

- **Escalating deposits** -- spending more over time, especially after losses
- **Chasing losses** -- increasing bets to recover previous losses
- **Increasing session length and frequency** -- playing longer and more often than their baseline
- **Repeated limit increases** -- raising self-imposed limits shortly after hitting them
- **Late-night play spikes** -- sessions shifting to unusual hours
- **Out-of-pattern behavior** -- sudden changes in stake size, product, or frequency

**No single indicator is definitive.** Patterns matter. A one-time large bet during the Super Bowl is normal. A sustained increase in stake size over 3 weeks is a signal. Context is everything.

> Start at Level 1. Move to Level 2 when you have the data. Level 3 is a long-term goal. Most operators launch successfully with structural identification alone -- your product touchpoints already tell you more than you think.

## Section 4: Adapting Messages

### Reading

The same core message adapts to different segments by changing tone, depth, and framing -- not the underlying information. The fact doesn't change. The lens does.

**Rules for adapting:**

1. **Same fact, different depth.** New/Novice needs the definition. Recreational needs the comparison. Enthusiast needs the advanced angle. The underlying truth is identical.
2. **Match their language.** Sports bettors say "vig," not "house edge." Poker players say "rake." Casino players know "RTP." Use the vocabulary of their vertical.
3. **Respect what they already know.** Don't explain house edge to an Enthusiast. Don't explain basic odds to someone who's been playing for 3 years. Add to their knowledge, don't start from zero.
4. **At-Risk and In-Crisis get tools, not education.** When behavioral indicators suggest players in difficulty, more information isn't the answer. Tool visibility and support access are.
5. **The general voice works for most content.** Only adapt when the touchpoint, campaign, or behavioral data demands it. Your default is always the general Playbook voice.

### Segment Movement Patterns

Segments aren't permanent labels -- they describe where a player is *right now*, not who they are. Players move between segments as their knowledge grows, their habits change, or life circumstances shift. Your content strategy needs to account for these transitions, not just the states themselves.

Each transition has a **trigger** (the behavioral signal that tells you something is changing) and a **content response** (how your messaging should adapt). The goal is to meet the player where they're heading, not where they were.

**New/Novice -> Recreational**
- **Trigger:** Time + knowledge. The player learns how the product works, develops consistent patterns.
- **Response:** Gradually reduce "how it works" content. Introduce engagement content, myth-busting, social features.

**Recreational -> Enthusiast**
- **Trigger:** Deepening engagement, increasing knowledge, multi-vertical exploration.
- **Response:** Offer advanced content. Don't repeat basics. Treat them as a peer, not a student.

**Any -> At-Risk**
- **Trigger:** Behavioral indicators: escalating deposits, chasing losses, increasing frequency, repeated limit changes.
- **Response:** Increase tool visibility. Gentle check-ins. Make support one tap away. Never label or diagnose.

**At-Risk -> In-Crisis**
- **Trigger:** Self-identification (clicking "I need help"), self-exclusion initiation, severe behavioral patterns.
- **Response:** Tier 2 response. Zero friction. Zero cleverness. Immediate access to support.

**At-Risk -> Recreational**
- **Trigger:** Patterns stabilize. Behavior returns to baseline. Tools are working.
- **Response:** Quietly reduce check-in frequency. Don't celebrate or reference the risk period. Resume normal content.

Notice that movement is **not always forward**. A Recreational player who encounters a stressful life event may shift toward At-Risk, then return to Recreational when things stabilize. An Enthusiast who tries a new product vertical drops back to New/Novice for that specific product. The model is fluid by design -- it responds to behavior, not biography.

> The most important content moments happen during segment transitions -- when a New/Novice is becoming Recreational, or when a Recreational player starts showing At-Risk signals. Build content for these inflection points, not just steady states. If you only have content for "where they are," you'll always be one step behind.

## Key Takeaways

- **Six segments, relationship-based:** New/Novice, Recreational, Enthusiast, At-Risk, In-Crisis, Friends & Family -- defined by knowledge, engagement, and behavior, not demographics
- **Product-contextual:** A player can be Recreational in casino and New/Novice in sports simultaneously -- segment is tied to a specific product
- **Dynamic:** Players move between segments as behavior changes -- design content for transitions, not just steady states
- **Five verticals overlay independently:** Sports, Casino, Lottery, Poker, Bingo -- each has its own knowledge domains and misconceptions
- **Three identification levels:** Structural (no data needed), basic data, behavioral analytics -- start at Level 1 and build up
- **Adapt the lens, not the fact:** The same core message changes by tone, depth, and vocabulary depending on segment and vertical -- but the underlying truth stays constant
- **General voice is your default:** Only adapt for segment-specific touchpoints, targeted campaigns, or behavioral data matches
