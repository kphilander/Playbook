# Recognizing Escalation Signals

This module teaches you to read the signals that tell you a conversation has crossed from Tier 1 to Tier 2. Most player interactions are straightforward — a question about tools, a request for odds information, a deposit limit adjustment. But some interactions carry signals that something deeper is happening. Recognizing those signals early is the difference between a player who gets the help they need and one who doesn't.

After completing this module, you will be able to:

3. Describe the at-risk player profile — behavioral signals, self-perception, and content receptivity
4. Correctly identify whether a given scenario requires Tier 1 or Tier 2 handling

## Section 1: The Four Signal Categories

### Reading

Escalation signals fall into four categories. No single signal is a diagnosis — you are not a counselor, and it is not your job to determine what a player is experiencing. Your job is to recognize patterns and respond appropriately.

**Category 1: Emotional signals.** These are the most visible. A player who is crying, having angry outbursts, expressing despair, or showing signs of acute distress is signaling that this is not a routine interaction. Emotional signals are often the clearest indicator that you need to shift from Tier 1 to Tier 2.

**Category 2: Financial signals.** A player who mentions they can't afford rent or bills, asks to borrow money, requests emergency withdrawals, or describes financial stress connected to their gambling is communicating something beyond a routine transaction. Financial signals often come embedded in what sounds like a normal request — "I need to withdraw everything right now" might be routine, or it might be urgent.

**Category 3: Behavioral signals.** These are harder to detect in a single interaction because they involve patterns over time: prolonged play without breaks, rapidly increasing deposits, chasing losses aggressively, or exceeding self-set limits repeatedly. If your system surfaces behavioral data (account history, deposit patterns, limit changes), look at it. If it doesn't, listen for the player telling you indirectly — "I've been playing all night" or "I just keep depositing more."

**Category 4: Verbal signals.** Specific phrases are high-priority indicators. "I can't stop." "I've lost everything." "I don't know what to do." "What's the point." These phrases — especially anything referencing self-harm, hopelessness, or feeling trapped — require immediate Tier 2 handling. If a player mentions self-harm or suicidal thoughts, follow your organization's crisis protocol. No exceptions.

Here is a reference table adapted from the [Staff FAQ]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/collateral/customer-service/staff-faq.md):

| Category | Example signals |
|----------|----------------|
| **Emotional** | Crying, angry outbursts, expressing despair, audible distress |
| **Financial** | Can't afford bills, asks to borrow money, mentions financial crisis |
| **Behavioral** | Prolonged play without breaks, chasing losses, repeated limit increases |
| **Verbal** | "I can't stop," "I've lost everything," "I don't know what to do" |
| **Crisis** | Mentions self-harm, expresses hopelessness, references suicide |

**The crisis category sits above all others.** If you detect crisis signals — any mention of self-harm, suicidal thoughts, or expressions of hopelessness — activate your organization's crisis protocol immediately. Do not attempt to handle this yourself. Stay on the line, acknowledge the person, and connect them to crisis resources (988 or local equivalent).

> **Operator integration point**: Your organization's crisis protocol is specific to your workplace. Complete your operator's crisis training in addition to this module. Configure `_brand.yml` → `integrations.internal_policies` with your internal escalation procedures URL.

## Section 2: Frustrated vs. Distressed

### Reading

The most important distinction in Tier 2 support is telling the difference between a frustrated customer and a player in genuine distress. They can look similar on the surface — both may raise their voice, use strong language, or make demands. But the response they need is entirely different.

**A frustrated customer** has a specific problem they want solved. Their issue is with the product, the process, or the service — not with their own situation. "This is ridiculous — why can't I remove my deposit limit?" is frustration. The player disagrees with a policy. They want a specific outcome. The emotional energy is directed outward, at you or the system.

**A player in distress** is expressing something about their own state. The issue is not a broken feature or a slow process — it's how they feel. "I don't know what to do anymore" is distress. "I just can't stop" is distress. "I've ruined everything" is distress. The emotional energy is directed inward, at themselves and their situation.

Here's how to tell the difference:

| Dimension | Frustrated customer | Player in distress |
|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Focus** | The problem (product, policy, process) | Themselves (feelings, situation, behavior) |
| **Language** | "Why can't you..." / "This is unacceptable" / "I want to speak to a manager" | "I can't stop" / "I don't know what to do" / "I've lost everything" |
| **Goal** | A specific resolution (fix this, override that, escalate) | May not have a clear goal — may just need to be heard |
| **Emotional direction** | Outward — at you, the company, the system | Inward — at themselves, their situation |
| **What they need** | A solution, an explanation, or an escalation path | Someone to listen. Then: the helpline, an account pause, or a referral. |

**Why this matters**: If you handle a distressed player like a frustrated customer — trying to "solve" their problem with process steps — you miss the moment. And if you handle a frustrated customer like a distressed player — going soft and offering the helpline — you'll come across as condescending.

Read the direction of the emotion. Outward means frustration. Inward means distress. That one observation changes your entire approach.

**What about both?** Sometimes a player is frustrated *and* distressed. They're angry about a policy but also revealing something deeper. When signals overlap, default to the Tier 2 response. It is always safe to be warm. It is never safe to be glib.

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Here's the skill that separates good support agents from great ones: telling the difference between a frustrated customer and a player in genuine distress.
>
> [show comparison table] Both can sound angry. Both can be demanding. Both can use strong language. But look at where the emotion is pointed.
>

>
> [pause] The frustrated customer needs a solution. The distressed player needs to be heard first. If you try to solve when someone needs to be heard, you miss the moment entirely.

## Section 3: The At-Risk Player Profile

### Reading

The {{PROGRAM_NAME}} [at-risk player segment]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/messaging/player-segments/at-risk-players.md) describes players whose behavioral patterns have shifted. This is not a demographic category — it is defined entirely by behavior. Understanding this profile helps you recognize Tier 2 signals before they escalate to crisis.

**Who they are**: Players whose play patterns have changed — increased frequency, higher deposits, chasing losses, exceeding self-set limits, longer sessions, reduced breaks between sessions. They are identified by behavioral data, not by self-report.

**How they see themselves**: "I'm fine. I just had a bad run." / "I'm about to turn it around." Most players in this group do not recognize themselves as needing support. They would not click on anything labeled "at risk" or "get help." This is critical — it means direct confrontation will fail.

**Their emotional state**: Ranges from frustration to determination to genuine unawareness. Some are in denial. Others genuinely believe their current pattern is temporary.

**Content receptivity**: Low for anything that feels like a warning. Moderate for information framed as features. This means that if you approach with concern — "I've noticed your deposits have increased" — you will trigger defensiveness. If you approach with tools — "Did you know you can review your activity in the dashboard?" — you may get engagement.

**The rule for Tier 2 agents**: You never label this player. The phrase "at-risk" never appears in any player-facing conversation. Neither does "problem," "concern," "warning," or "we noticed." You provide tools, you make resources visible, and you let the player draw their own conclusions. If they open up, you listen. If they don't, you respect that.

**Where this profile meets Tier 2**: Most interactions with at-risk players stay in Tier 1 — tool promotion, feature descriptions, activity summaries. The interaction crosses to Tier 2 when the player reveals something about their state: they express distress, mention financial difficulty, say something that triggers your escalation signals. At that point, you shift — warm, direct, no humor — and follow the Tier 2 conversation approach you'll learn in Module 01.

### Narrator Script

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Let's talk about a specific player profile that sits right at the Tier 1/Tier 2 boundary.
>
> [show segment overview] The at-risk player segment describes people whose behavioral patterns have shifted — more frequent play, higher deposits, chasing losses. But here's the key: most of them don't see it that way. They think they're fine. They think the bad run is temporary.
>
> [pause] So what does this mean for you? It means you never label them. The word "at-risk" never leaves your mouth. You don't say "we noticed" or "we're concerned." That's surveillance language, and it triggers defensiveness instantly.
>
> [show approach diagram] Instead, you make tools visible. You mention the activity dashboard. You let the data speak for itself. And if the player opens up — if they move from "I'm fine" to "I don't know what's happening" — that's when you shift to Tier 2.
>
> [pause] The skill here is patience. You can't force someone to see what they're not ready to see. But you can make sure the door is open when they are.
>

### Question 5

**Assesses:** Learning objective 4

**Stem:** A player calls and says quietly: "I just... I don't know anymore. My wife left. I've been up all night playing. I don't know why I'm calling you." What is the correct response?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| A | Tier 1 — ask the player what specific tool or feature they need help with |
| B | Tier 2 — acknowledge what they've shared, listen, then offer the helpline and an account pause |
| C | Tier 2 — immediately transfer the player to the helpline without further conversation |
| D | Tier 1 — suggest the player review their activity dashboard to see their spending patterns |

**Correct:** B

**Explanation:** Multiple Tier 2 signals are present: emotional distress (quiet voice, uncertainty), behavioral (up all night playing), and verbal ("I don't know anymore"). The correct response is to listen first, acknowledge ("I hear you, and I'm glad you called"), then offer the helpline and the option to pause their account. Do not transfer without connecting first, and do not redirect to Tier 1 tools — this is not the time for feature promotion.

## Key Takeaways

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Let's recap what you've learned.

- **Four signal categories**: Emotional, financial, behavioral, and verbal. Crisis signals (self-harm, suicidal thoughts) override everything else and trigger your crisis protocol immediately.
- **Frustrated vs. distressed**: Read the direction of the emotion. Outward (at the system) means frustration. Inward (at themselves) means distress. When in doubt, default to Tier 2.
- **The at-risk player profile**: Defined by behavioral patterns, not demographics. These players don't self-identify. Never label them. Make tools visible and let the player lead.
- **You are not a counselor.** You recognize signals, adjust your approach, listen with empathy, and connect people to the right resources. That is the job, and it is enough.

> **Narrator:** Next up is Module 01: Tier 2 Conversation Skills. That's where you'll learn how to actually have these conversations — the voice shift, the de-escalation techniques, and exactly what to say (and what never to say) when a player is in distress. See you there.
