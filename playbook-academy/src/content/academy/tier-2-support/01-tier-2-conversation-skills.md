This module teaches you what to do next — the voice shift, the conversation structure, the de-escalation techniques, and the words you should never use. You'll walk through two complete scenarios from the {{PROGRAM_NAME}} [Conversation Scripts]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/collateral/customer-service/conversation-scripts.md): a player in emotional distress and a player disputing a limit they set.

## Section 1: The Voice Shift

### Reading

In Module 01 of the All Employees program, you learned that {{PROGRAM_NAME}} has two tiers. Tier 1 is entertainment literacy — confident, witty, sometimes cheeky. Tier 2 is support — warm, direct, and completely humor-free. The same brand voice powers both, but the tone shifts significantly.

Here are the five dimensions of that shift, drawn from the [Voice and Tone guide]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/brand-book/04-voice-and-tone.md#tier-1-vs-tier-2-tone):

| Dimension | Tier 1 | Tier 2 |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| **Energy** | Confident, energetic, witty | Calm, steady, warm |
| **Humor** | Appropriate — cheeky, self-aware | Never — empathy only |
| **Sentence length** | 15-20 words average | 10-15 words average (shorter under stress) |
| **CTAs** | "Take the quiz" / "See the odds" | "Call now" / "You're not alone" |
| **Helpline framing** | "For any question about gambling" | "Free, confidential, 24/7" |

**The shift happens in your voice, not on a settings page.** There is no button to press. When you recognize that a conversation has crossed from Tier 1 to Tier 2, you change how you speak — slower, warmer, shorter sentences. You drop the clever phrases. You stop promoting features. You become a person helping another person.

**What the Tier 2 voice sounds like in practice:**

- Short sentences. Maximum 15 words.
- Simple words. No jargon. No euphemisms.
- Direct structure. Subject-verb-object.
- Second person. "You can..." not "Players may..."
- Present tense. "Support is available now." Not "Support will be available."

**The critical rule**: When in doubt about whether a moment is Tier 1 or Tier 2, go with Tier 2. It is always safe to be warm. It is never safe to be glib.

### Narrator Script

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] You know how to spot the signals. Now let's talk about what happens next — the voice shift.
>
> [show comparison table] Here's what changes. Your energy goes from confident to calm. Humor drops to zero. Your sentences get shorter. Your CTAs shift from "take the quiz" to "you're not alone."
>
> [pause] This isn't a script change. It's a mode change. You're still the same person. But you're showing a different side — the side that listens, that cares, that doesn't rush.
>
> [show voice characteristics] In practice: short sentences, simple words, direct structure. "Support is available now." Not "Why don't you check out our comprehensive suite of support options." The player doesn't need comprehensive. They need clear.
>
> [transition to scenario] Let's see this in action with a real scenario.

## Section 2: Scenario Walkthrough — Player in Distress

### Reading

This walkthrough follows [Scenario 4]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/collateral/customer-service/conversation-scripts.md#scenario-4--player-in-emotional-distress) from the Conversation Scripts — a player in emotional distress. This is the highest-priority Tier 2 scenario.

**The framework is: Listen, Acknowledge, Offer.**

**Step 1: Listen.** Let the player talk. Do not interrupt. Do not jump to solutions. Do not insert scripted language while they are speaking. Silence on your end is not failure — it is respect.

**Step 2: Acknowledge.** After they have spoken, validate that you heard them. Not with a diagnosis. Not with reassurance you can't back up. With honest acknowledgment.

Say: "I hear you, and I'm glad you called. Whatever's going on, you don't have to figure it out alone."

Say: "Thank you for sharing that with me. I want to make sure you get the right support."

Do NOT say: "Calm down." Do NOT say: "It's going to be okay." Do NOT say: "It sounds like you have a problem." You don't know those things. Don't claim them.

**Step 3: Offer.** Once you've listened and acknowledged, offer two things:

1. **The helpline**: "There's a free, confidential service that specializes in this. You can talk to someone right now by calling {{HELPLINE_NUMBER}}. They're available 24/7, and there's no judgment."
2. **An account pause**: "I can help you pause your account right now. Would you like me to do that?"

If the player wants to pause, process it immediately. Do not add friction. Do not ask "Are you sure?" The player has already decided.

**If the player mentions self-harm or suicidal thoughts**: This activates your organization's crisis protocol. The steps are typically: stay on the line, acknowledge ("I'm here with you"), refer to crisis line (988 or local equivalent), log the interaction, follow up per policy. Do not transfer to a queue. Do not put them on hold.

**Closing**: "You've done a brave thing by calling. The helpline is there whenever you need it — {{HELPLINE_NUMBER}}. And I'm here if you need anything else from us."

## Section 3: Scenario Walkthrough — Limit Dispute

### Reading

This walkthrough follows [Scenario 5]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/collateral/customer-service/conversation-scripts.md#scenario-5--player-disputes-a-limit-they-set) from the Conversation Scripts — a player who wants to remove or override a limit they previously set.

This scenario is tricky because the player is not in distress — at least not obviously. They have a specific request ("remove my limit") and they may push back hard when you explain the cooling-off period. The challenge is to be firm without being patronizing, and clear without being adversarial.

**The approach**:

1. **Acknowledge the request.** "I understand. Let me pull up your account and take a look."
2. **Explain the process factually.** "Your deposit limit is currently set to [amount] per [period]. You set this on [date]. To change it, there's a cooling-off period of {{COOL_OFF_PERIOD}}."
3. **Frame the cooling-off period as design, not punishment.** "The cooling-off period is designed to protect the choice you made when you set the limit. It applies to everyone." This is the key language — "the choice you made" reinforces their agency. "It applies to everyone" removes the personal sting.
4. **If they push back**, stay patient: "I hear you, and I understand this isn't what you wanted to hear. The cooling-off period is a standard feature for all limit changes. I can't speed it up, but I can help you with anything else."

**What NOT to say:**

| Don't say | Why |
|-----------|-----|
| "It's for your own good" | Patronizing. You're not their parent. |
| "We can't let you do that" | Adversarial. Makes it sound like you're blocking them. |
| "Are you sure you want to remove it?" | Questions their judgment. It's their decision. |
| "Maybe you should keep it" | Prescriptive. Not your call. |

**When a limit dispute becomes Tier 2**: If the player transitions from frustration to distress — if the language shifts from "This is ridiculous" to "I can't handle this" — you shift too. Drop the process explanation and move to the listen-acknowledge-offer framework from Scenario 4.

### Narrator Script

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Scenario 5: a player disputes a limit they set. This one tests a different skill — being firm and clear without being patronizing.
>
> [show approach steps] Start by acknowledging the request. Then explain the process — factually, not defensively. Frame the cooling-off period as a design feature, not a restriction: "It's designed to protect the choice you made."
>
> [show key phrases] Notice the language. "The choice you made" — that's agency. "Standard feature for all players" — that's fairness. "I can't speed it up" — that's honesty.
>
> [show what not to say] And here's what you never say. Not "It's for your own good." Not "Are you sure?" Not "Maybe you should keep it." Every one of those phrases takes away the player's autonomy. You're there to explain the process, not to judge the decision.
>
> [pause] But watch for the shift. If the player goes from "this is ridiculous" to "I just can't handle this anymore" — they've crossed from frustrated to distressed. And you need to cross with them.
>
> [transition to de-escalation] Now let's talk about what to do when the temperature rises.

## Section 4: De-escalation and What Not to Say

### Reading

Sometimes a Tier 2 conversation gets tense. The player is angry, upset, or pushing back hard. De-escalation is not about winning the argument or making the player comply. It's about lowering the emotional temperature so the player can hear you.

**Three de-escalation techniques:**

**Technique 1: Name the emotion, don't fight it.** "I can hear this is frustrating." "I understand this isn't what you wanted to hear." Naming the emotion tells the player you're listening. It does not mean you agree with their demand. It means you see them as a person, not a problem to solve.

**Technique 2: Separate the person from the policy.** "The cooling-off period is a standard feature. It applies to all players." This is not about them. It's about the system. Removing the personal dimension lowers defensiveness. When a player hears "I can't let you do that," they hear an adversary. When they hear "It applies to everyone," they hear a fact.

**Technique 3: Offer what you can.** Even when you can't give the player what they want, offer something. "I can't speed up the cooling-off period, but I can submit the request now so it starts immediately. And I can help you with anything else in the meantime." Having *something* to offer keeps the conversation moving forward instead of hitting a wall.

**The absolute "never say" list for Tier 2:**

| Never say | Why it fails |
|-----------|-------------|
| "Calm down" | Invalidates their feelings. Has never once calmed anyone down. |
| "It's going to be okay" | You don't know that. False reassurance erodes trust. |
| "It sounds like you have a problem" | You are not a counselor. Do not diagnose. |
| "You should talk to someone" (as an opener) | Don't lead with the helpline. Listen first. |
| "Have you considered setting a limit?" | Not the time for tool promotion. The player is in distress. |
| "Gamble responsibly" | Generic, actionless, and exactly the kind of language {{PROGRAM_NAME}} exists to replace. |
| "It's for your own good" | Patronizing. Removes agency. |
| "There's nothing I can do" | There is always something — a referral, a pause, a number. |
| Clinical terms: "pathological," "addicted," "disordered" | Clinical language is for clinicians. You are not one. |

## Section 5: When to Escalate Beyond Your Role

### Reading

You're trained in Tier 2 conversation skills — listening, acknowledging, de-escalating, and connecting people to the right resources. But you are not a counselor, a legal advisor, or a crisis specialist. Knowing when a situation goes beyond your training is as important as knowing how to respond within it.

**When to escalate:**

- **Self-harm or suicide mention** — immediate. Follow your organization's crisis protocol. Do not attempt to counsel the caller yourself. Stay on the line, acknowledge them, and connect them to crisis services (988 or local equivalent).
- **Threats of violence** — toward the caller, toward others, or toward staff. Follow your security protocol immediately.
- **Legal threats or demands** — "I'm going to sue," requests involving lawyers, power of attorney disputes that go beyond the standard privacy boundary. Route to your legal or compliance team.
- **Callers who need clinical assessment** — when someone describes symptoms, asks for a diagnosis, or needs therapeutic support that goes beyond a helpline referral. You are not equipped to assess clinical needs.
- **Situations where you feel unsafe or out of your depth** — trust that feeling. If you don't know what to say next, or if the conversation has moved into territory you haven't been trained for, that is your signal to bring in someone who has.

**How to hand off without re-traumatizing the caller:**

The worst thing you can do during an escalation is make the caller start over. They've already told you something difficult. Making them repeat it to someone new adds friction and erodes trust.

Use this language: "I want to make sure you get the best possible help. I'm going to connect you with [supervisor/specialist] who can help with exactly this situation."

Before you transfer, brief the next person yourself. Give them the context: what the caller said, what you've already covered, what the caller needs. The caller should not have to retell their story from the beginning.

If a warm handoff isn't possible — if you need to transfer to a queue — tell the caller what to expect: "You may need to wait briefly. The person you'll speak with specializes in this, and I've noted what we've discussed so they'll have the background."

| B | "Your limit is set to [amount] per [period]. To remove it, there's a cooling-off period of {{COOL_OFF_PERIOD}}. That's a standard feature for all limit changes. I can submit the request now." |
| C | "Sorry, I can't help with that. Limits are permanent once set." |
| D | "I think you should keep the limit — it's protecting you." |

**Correct:** B

**Explanation:** Explain the process factually, frame the cooling-off period as a standard feature, and offer to submit the request. Option A questions their judgment, Option C is inaccurate and unhelpful, and Option D is prescriptive. You explain the system — you don't judge the decision.

### Question 4

**Assesses:** Learning objective 4

**Stem:** A player is becoming increasingly agitated about a policy. They've raised their voice and said: "This is the worst customer service I've ever experienced." Which de-escalation technique should you apply first?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| A | Match their energy level to show you take the issue seriously |
| B | Name the emotion: "I can hear this is frustrating, and I understand it's not what you were hoping to hear." |
| C | Immediately transfer to a supervisor to avoid further escalation |
| D | Tell them: "I need you to calm down before we can continue this conversation." |

**Correct:** B

**Explanation:** Naming the emotion tells the player you're listening without agreeing to their demand or fighting back. "I can hear this is frustrating" validates their experience. Matching energy escalates. Telling them to calm down invalidates their feelings. Transferring without trying to de-escalate abandons the conversation.

### Question 5

**Assesses:** Learning objective 5

**Stem:** Which of the following phrases should never be used in a Tier 2 conversation?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| A | "I hear you, and I'm glad you called." |
| B | "Free, confidential support is available right now." |
| C | "It sounds like you might have a gambling addiction. Have you thought about getting help?" |
| D | "Would you like me to pause your account? I can do that right now." |

**Correct:** C

**Explanation:** "Gambling addiction" is a clinical term. Diagnosing a player is not your role. The phrase "It sounds like you might have..." is a diagnostic statement that will trigger defensiveness, shame, or both. Options A, B, and D are all appropriate Tier 2 language — warm, direct, and non-clinical.

**Source:** [Staff FAQ — Language and Communication]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/collateral/customer-service/staff-faq.md#language-and-communication)

## Key Takeaways

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Let's recap.

- **The Tier 2 voice shift** is about tone, not topic. Calm, warm, direct. Short sentences. No humor. No cleverness.
- **Listen, Acknowledge, Offer** — in that order. Listen without interrupting. Acknowledge without diagnosing. Offer the helpline and an account pause.
- **Limit disputes** require firmness without patronizing. Frame the cooling-off period as a standard design feature. "The choice you made" reinforces agency.
- **De-escalation works in three steps**: name the emotion, separate the person from the policy, offer what you can.
- **The never-say list exists for a reason.** "Calm down," clinical terms, false reassurance, and diagnostic statements all do damage. When in doubt, listen more and say less.
- **Know when to escalate.** Self-harm mentions, threats, legal demands, and clinical needs go beyond your training. Hand off without making the caller repeat their story — brief the next person yourself.
