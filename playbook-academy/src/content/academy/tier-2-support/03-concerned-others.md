# Supporting Concerned Others

This module teaches you to handle a category of interaction that most support agents are unprepared for: when someone contacts you about someone else. A spouse worried about their partner's gambling. A parent who noticed their child's spending. A friend who doesn't know what to do. These callers are not players. They need different content, different tone, and different resources. This module covers all of it — including the privacy boundary that governs every word you say.

## Section 1: The Friends-and-Family Audience

### Reading

The [friends-and-family segment]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/messaging/player-segments/friends-and-family.md) is one of the most overlooked audiences in the gambling industry. These are not players. They are partners, parents, children, siblings, and friends of someone whose gambling concerns them. They need fundamentally different content than what you provide to players.

**Who they are:**

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Who** | Partners, parents, children, siblings, friends of someone whose gambling worries them |
| **State of mind** | Worried, frustrated, unsure, possibly exhausted from trying to help |
| **What they need** | Recognition that their concern is valid. Practical guidance. Support for themselves — not just for the person they're worried about. |
| **What they don't need** | Diagnostic checklists. Advice on how to "fix" the other person. Tools designed for players (deposit limits, dashboards). |
| **Content receptivity** | High for practical guidance. Low for clinical checklists. Zero for player-facing content repurposed for families. |

**What makes this audience different:**

1. **They can't control the situation.** A player can set a limit, pause their account, or call the helpline. A concerned family member can do none of those things for someone else. This feeling of powerlessness defines their experience.

2. **They need support for themselves.** Worrying about someone's gambling is stressful and exhausting. Concerned others need their own support resources — not just referrals for the person they're worried about. When you tell a worried parent "here's a helpline for your son," you've missed half the need. They need a helpline too.

3. **They need conversation guidance, not ultimatum scripts.** Most concerned others want to talk to their loved one but don't know how. They want practical scripts — what to say, what not to say, how to start the conversation without triggering defensiveness. They are not looking for "tough love" strategies or intervention playbooks.

4. **They are often the first point of contact.** In many cases, the concerned family member reaches out before the player does. This makes them a critical audience for Tier 2 support — they may be the bridge that eventually connects the player to help.

>

## Section 2: The Privacy Boundary

### Reading

This is the rule that governs every interaction with a concerned other:

**You cannot share any details about another person's account — including whether they have one.**

This is not a guideline. It is a privacy requirement. It applies regardless of the caller's relationship to the player, regardless of how worried they are, and regardless of how convincing their request sounds.

**What you cannot do:**

- Confirm or deny that someone has an account
- Share account activity, deposit history, or session data
- Disclose whether someone has set limits, used tools, or self-excluded
- Make changes to someone else's account (close it, set limits, pause it)
- Share any personal information about an account holder

**What you can do:**

- Listen to the caller's concerns with empathy and warmth
- Provide support resources — the helpline, Gam-Anon, financial counseling
- Explain what tools are generally available to players (without confirming whether this specific player has used them)
- Validate their concern without confirming or denying the account

**How to communicate the boundary:**

This is where most agents struggle. Saying "I can't help you" feels dismissive. Saying "I can't share that information" feels cold. The skill is to hold the boundary while remaining warm and helpful.

The [Conversation Scripts]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/collateral/customer-service/conversation-scripts.md#scenario-6--concerned-family-member-calls) provide this language:

"I should let you know upfront — for privacy reasons, I'm not able to share any details about another person's account, including whether they have one. But I can absolutely help you with resources and next steps."

Notice the structure: **boundary + redirect**. You state the limit clearly, then immediately pivot to what you *can* do. This avoids the dead-end feeling. The caller doesn't hear "no" — they hear "not that, but this."

**When the caller pushes back:**

Some callers will push. "But I'm their spouse." "But this is an emergency." "But I have power of attorney." The boundary does not change. Your response:

"I understand, and I can hear how important this is to you. But for privacy reasons, I'm not able to share account information regardless of the relationship. What I can do is connect you with resources that can help you figure out your next steps."

**The one exception**: If the caller describes imminent danger — the player is threatening self-harm, or the caller is in physical danger — follow your organization's crisis protocol. Contact emergency services if needed. This is not an account-information exception; it is a safety exception.

>

## Section 3: Scenario Walkthrough — Concerned Family Member Calls

### Reading

This walkthrough follows [Scenario 6]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/collateral/customer-service/conversation-scripts.md#scenario-6--concerned-family-member-calls) from the Conversation Scripts. A family member calls about their loved one's gambling behavior.

**The framework: Validate, Boundary, Resource.**

**Step 1: Validate.** Acknowledge their concern. They called because they care, and that matters.

"Thank you for reaching out. I can hear this is important to you, and I want to make sure I point you to the right resources."

**Step 2: Boundary.** Set the privacy expectation early. Don't wait until they ask a specific question — tell them upfront.

"I should let you know upfront — for privacy reasons, I'm not able to share any details about another person's account, including whether they have one. But I can absolutely help you with resources and next steps."

**Step 3: Resource.** Provide specific, actionable resources — for the player and for the caller.

"The best resource I can offer is the helpline: {{HELPLINE_NUMBER}}. They specialize in supporting people who are worried about someone's gambling. It's free, confidential, and available 24/7. And it's for you too — not just for players."

**Common requests and how to handle them:**

| Request | Response |
|---------|----------|
| "Can you close my husband's account?" | "I can't make changes to another person's account — that has to come from the account holder. But the helpline can help you figure out how to approach that conversation." |
| "Can you at least tell me if they have an account here?" | "For privacy reasons, I can't share that. But the helpline at {{HELPLINE_NUMBER}} can help you understand your options regardless." |
| "What should I do?" | "I'm not a counselor, so I don't want to give you advice outside my expertise. But the helpline has trained counselors who specialize in exactly this. They can walk you through your options." |

**What NOT to say:**

| Don't say | Why |
|-----------|-----|
| "Your family member may have a problem" | Don't diagnose — you don't know the situation. |
| "We'll look into their account" | Privacy violation. |
| "There's nothing we can do" | There is something — referral to the helpline and family support resources. |
| "They need to want to change" | Not your place. Leave that for counselors. |

**Closing:**

"Thank you for caring enough to call. The helpline number again is {{HELPLINE_NUMBER}} — free, confidential, 24/7. They're there for you too, not just for players."

### Narrator Script

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Let's walk through the full conversation. A family member calls. They're worried. They want answers you can't give and action you can't take. Here's how to handle it.
>
> [show framework] Three steps: validate, boundary, resource.
>
> [show step 1] Validate first. "Thank you for reaching out. I can hear this is important to you." They called because they care. Acknowledge that before anything else.
>
> [show step 2] Boundary next — and set it early. "For privacy reasons, I can't share details about another person's account." Don't wait until they ask. Tell them upfront so they know the playing field.
>
> [show step 3] Resource last. The helpline. Gam-Anon. Financial counseling if needed. And make sure they know: "The helpline is for you too — not just for players." Many concerned others don't realize they can call for themselves.
>
> [show closing] End with warmth: "Thank you for caring enough to call." This person took a brave step. Recognize it.
>

## Section 4: Support Resources for Concerned Others

### Reading

Concerned others need their own resource set. Do not simply repeat the player-facing helpline — acknowledge that the resources serve them too, and add family-specific organizations.

**Resource set for concerned others:**

| Resource | What it provides | How to present it |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Helpline** ({{HELPLINE_NUMBER}}) | Trained counselors who support concerned others — not just players | "The helpline is for you too. They specialize in helping people who are worried about someone's gambling." |
| **Gam-Anon** (gam-anon.org) | Support groups specifically for families and friends of people who gamble | "There are support groups specifically for families. Gam-Anon has meetings where you can connect with people going through similar situations." |
| **Financial counseling** | Debt management, budgeting, financial recovery | "If gambling has created financial stress in your household, a financial counselor can help you understand your options." |
| **Text/chat support** | Non-phone options for concerned others who prefer text | "You can also text {{TEXT_NUMBER}} or chat at {{CHAT_URL}} if you'd prefer not to call." |

**Three things to always communicate to a concerned other:**

1. **"The helpline is for you too."** Many people assume helplines only serve players. Telling them explicitly that the helpline supports concerned others removes a barrier.

2. **"You don't have to figure this out alone."** Concerned others often feel isolated. Acknowledging that they can get help reframes the situation — they are not just a messenger for the player.

3. **"This is hard, and you're doing the right thing by reaching out."** Validation matters. A concerned other who feels dismissed will not call again. One who feels heard may become the bridge that connects the player to help.

## Section 5: Taking Care of Yourself

### Reading

You've spent this module — and this entire program — learning to hold space for other people's pain. Concerned family members in distress. Players in crisis. Callers who are angry, scared, or exhausted. That takes a toll. This section is about you.

**Post-call decompression**: After a distressing call, step away for 5 minutes. Stand up. Walk to the water cooler. Look out a window. Even a 2-minute reset between calls helps. The goal is to create a clear boundary between one interaction and the next so you don't carry the last caller's distress into the next caller's experience.

**Debriefing**: Talk to a colleague or supervisor — even briefly. "That was a tough one" is enough to start. You don't need a formal session. You need to say it out loud to another person instead of holding it inside. Don't carry it alone.

**Recognizing compassion fatigue**: When you absorb other people's distress repeatedly, it changes how you feel about the work. Signs to watch for:

- Difficulty disconnecting after calls — replaying conversations in your head
- Emotional numbness — not feeling much of anything during calls that should move you
- Dreading your shift or avoiding certain types of calls
- Physical symptoms: headaches, sleep disruption, fatigue that rest doesn't fix
- Becoming cynical about callers or dismissing their concerns more quickly

These are normal responses to absorbing others' distress. They are not signs of weakness or failure. They are signals that you need support.

**Seeking support**: Your organization should provide an Employee Assistance Program (EAP) or counseling service. Using it is not weakness — it is maintenance. You maintain the tools you use at work. Your wellbeing is one of those tools. The helpline serves concerned others, including people concerned about their own wellbeing.

## Key Takeaways

- **The privacy boundary is absolute.** You cannot share any account details about another person — including whether they have an account. No exceptions for relationships, legal documents, or emotional appeals.
- **Validate, Boundary, Resource** — in that order. Acknowledge their concern first. Set the privacy expectation early. Then connect them to the helpline, Gam-Anon, and financial counseling.
- **"The helpline is for you too."** Say this explicitly. Most concerned others don't know they can call for themselves.
- **You are not a counselor.** You listen, you validate, and you connect people to the right expertise. That is enough. That matters.
- **Take care of yourself.** Decompress after hard calls. Debrief with a colleague. Watch for signs of compassion fatigue. Use your organization's EAP or counseling service — that's maintenance, not weakness.

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] That's the end of the Tier 2 Support program. You've completed five modules: recognizing escalation signals, conversation skills, referral pathways, supporting concerned others, and taking care of yourself.
>
> These are the interactions that matter most. A player in distress who reaches a trained, empathetic agent gets a fundamentally different experience than one who reaches someone running through a script. You are now that trained agent.
>
> Recertification is every six months. The skills stay sharp with practice, and the helpline numbers and referral paths can change. Stay current.
>
> Thank you for taking this seriously. The players — and the people who care about them — are counting on you.
