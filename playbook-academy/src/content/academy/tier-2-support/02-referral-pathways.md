# Referral Pathways

This module teaches you where to send people and how to present each option naturally. You learned to recognize escalation signals in Module 00 and how to conduct the conversation in Module 01. Now you need to know the specific destinations — helplines, self-exclusion programs, cool-off periods, external organizations — and how to route each situation to the right one.

## Section 1: The Helpline Directory

### Reading

The helpline is the most important referral you will ever make. It connects players (and concerned others) to trained counselors who can provide the kind of support that is outside your role. Your job is to know the numbers, present them naturally, and make the connection feel easy — not clinical, not alarming, not like a last resort.

**The directory:**

| Channel | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Call** | {{HELPLINE_NUMBER}} |
| **Text** | {{TEXT_NUMBER}} |
| **Chat** | {{CHAT_URL}} |
| **Availability** | 24/7 |
| **Cost** | Free |
| **Privacy** | Confidential |

Memorize this. Not approximately — exactly. When a player is in distress, you don't want to be looking it up.

**How to present the helpline naturally:**

The difference between a good referral and a bad one is framing. The helpline should feel like a resource, not a warning. Here's the comparison from the [Staff FAQ]({{PLAYBOOK_REPO}}/blob/main/collateral/customer-service/staff-faq.md#language-and-communication):

**Natural (good):** "Free, confidential support is available right now. You can call {{HELPLINE_NUMBER}}, text {{TEXT_NUMBER}}, or chat at {{CHAT_URL}}. They're there for exactly this kind of conversation."

**Warning-style (bad):** "If you or someone you know has a gambling problem, call {{HELPLINE_NUMBER}}."

The difference is audience scope and tone. The good version works for anyone — someone in crisis, someone who is curious, someone who just wants to talk. The bad version only works for someone who has already decided they have a "problem" — and most people haven't. It's a gatekeeper phrase that keeps people out instead of inviting them in.

**Three rules for helpline presentation:**

1. **Always offer multiple channels.** Not everyone wants to call. Some players prefer text. Others prefer chat. Give them the choice.
2. **Pair it with "free, confidential, 24/7."** These three words remove the biggest barriers — cost, privacy, and availability.
3. **Don't lead with it.** In a distress conversation, listen and acknowledge first. The helpline comes in the "offer" step, not the opening line. The exception: when someone directly asks for support resources, give them immediately.

## Section 2: Cool-Off vs. Self-Exclusion

### Reading

Players can pause their account in two ways. They serve different purposes and have different mechanics. You need to understand both so you can explain them clearly and suggest the right one for the situation.

| Feature | Cool-off | Self-exclusion |
|---------|----------|----------------|
| **Duration** | 24 hours / 7 days / 30 days | 6 months / 1 year / 5 years / permanent |
| **Reversible?** | Account reactivates automatically when the period ends | Cannot be reversed during the exclusion period |
| **Marketing** | Paused during cool-off | Permanently removed during exclusion |
| **Terminology** | "Take a break" / "pause your account" | "Self-exclusion" (formal/Tier 2 contexts) |
| **Best for** | Short-term break, cooling off after a tough session, testing the experience | Longer commitment, more serious step, player wants full separation |

**When to mention cool-off:**

- Player says they want "a break" or "some time off"
- Player is mildly frustrated or wants to step away temporarily
- Player isn't sure how long they want to pause — start with the short option
- After a tough session when the player expresses wanting to stop for a while

**When to mention self-exclusion:**

- Player specifically asks about self-exclusion or "permanent" options
- Player has tried cool-off periods and keeps returning
- Player is in clear distress and wants full separation from the platform
- Player asks for "the longest option" or "something that can't be undone"

**How to present the options:**

Don't overwhelm the player with choices. Start by understanding what they want:

"Are you looking for a short break — a few days or weeks — or something longer?"

If short: "We have a cool-off option. You can pause for 24 hours, 7 days, or 30 days. When the period ends, your account becomes active again automatically."

If long: "We have a self-exclusion option for longer periods — 6 months, 1 year, 5 years, or permanent. During that time, your account is fully closed. You won't receive any marketing, and you can't reopen the account until the period ends."

If uncertain: "There's no pressure to decide right now. You can start with a short cool-off if you want to try it out, or go longer if that feels right. It's your call."

**Language note:** In Tier 1 contexts, use "pause your account" and "take a break." In Tier 2 contexts where the player has asked specifically about self-exclusion, you can use the formal term. Never use "ban yourself" — that's punitive framing.

> **Operator integration point**: Your self-exclusion enrollment process is operator-specific and may be regulated by your jurisdiction. Complete your operator's self-exclusion training in addition to this module. See `_brand.yml` → `integrations.self_exclusion_process`: {{SELF_EXCLUSION_PROCESS_URL}}.

## Section 3: External Support Organizations

### Reading

Your operator provides tools — deposit limits, session reminders, cool-off, self-exclusion. But some players need support that goes beyond what any operator can offer. That's where external organizations come in. You need to know what exists and when to mention it.

**Core external resources:**

| Organization | What it provides | Who it serves | How to mention it |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **National helpline** ({{HELPLINE_NUMBER}}) | Immediate phone/text/chat support from trained counselors | Players and concerned others | Primary referral for any Tier 2 situation |
| **Gamblers Anonymous (GA)** | Peer support groups, 12-step program | Players seeking community-based ongoing support | "There are free peer support groups that meet regularly" |
| **Gam-Anon** | Support groups specifically for families and friends | Concerned others | "There are groups specifically for people in your situation" |
| **National Council on Problem Gambling (NCPG)** | Helpline operation, treatment referral, education | Anyone affected by gambling | Typically the organization behind the helpline |
| **Financial counseling services** | Debt management, budgeting help, financial recovery | Players or families experiencing financial stress | "A financial counselor can help you understand your options" |

**Jurisdiction-specific resources:**

Self-exclusion registries vary by jurisdiction. Some are operator-level (your platform only), some are state/province-wide, and some are national. Examples:

- **GAMSTOP** (UK): National online self-exclusion scheme covering all licensed online operators
- **BetStop** (Australia): National self-exclusion register
- **State programs** (US): Individual state self-exclusion registries (vary by state)

Your operator should have configured the specific programs for your jurisdiction. Check `_brand.yml` → `integrations.self_exclusion_process` for the link to your enrollment process.

> **Operator integration point**: External clinical services — EAP programs, counseling partnerships, treatment referrals — are configured by your organization. See `_brand.yml` → `integrations.clinical_services`: {{CLINICAL_SERVICES_URL}}.

**When to mention external resources:**

- **Helpline**: Every Tier 2 conversation where the player shows distress, requests support, or asks about help. This is your default referral.
- **GA / peer support**: When a player mentions wanting ongoing community support, or has tried helpline-style support and wants something more regular.
- **Gam-Anon**: When a concerned family member calls (covered in detail in Module 03).
- **Financial counseling**: When a player or family member mentions financial stress — debt, rent, bills. Do not offer financial advice yourself.
- **Cross-operator self-exclusion**: When a player wants to exclude from all gambling, not just your platform.

**How to present external resources:**

The same principle as the helpline: natural, not clinical. Frame external organizations as specialized services, not last resorts.

"The helpline has trained counselors who specialize in exactly this."

"There are free peer support groups that meet regularly — people who understand what you're going through."

"A financial counselor can help you understand your options. That's their specialty."

Never say: "You need professional help." That implies the player is broken. Say: "There are people who specialize in this." That implies the player has access to expertise.

## Key Takeaways

> **Narrator:** [transition slide] Let's recap.

- **The helpline is your primary referral.** Memorize the numbers: call {{HELPLINE_NUMBER}}, text {{TEXT_NUMBER}}, chat {{CHAT_URL}}. Present it naturally — "free, confidential, 24/7" — not as a warning.
- **Cool-off is short-term.** 24 hours to 30 days. Auto-reactivates. Best for brief breaks. **Self-exclusion is long-term.** 6 months to permanent. Cannot be reversed. Best for players who want full separation.
- **External organizations fill the gaps** your operator can't cover: peer support (GA), family support (Gam-Anon), financial counseling, and cross-operator self-exclusion registries.
- **Match the referral to the situation.** Don't over-escalate and don't under-respond. A player asking for a week off gets a cool-off, not a self-exclusion. A player in crisis gets the helpline immediately, not a tool suggestion.
- **Frame resources as expertise, not rescue.** "There are people who specialize in this" opens doors. "You need professional help" closes them.
