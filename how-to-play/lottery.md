## The 30-second version

> Lottery is a pure-chance game. You pick numbers, a random draw picks numbers, and you hope they match. No skill, no strategy, no system changes the odds. Jackpot odds are astronomical — often worse than `1 in 100 million`. The overall RTP is typically `50–65%`, which is significantly lower than almost any casino game. Lotteries are entertainment with a dream attached, not an investment.

---

## How the game works

### Draw games

1. **Buy a ticket.** You select a set of numbers from a defined range — or let the machine pick them randomly (quick pick / lucky dip). Most draw games require choosing numbers from two separate pools (e.g., 5 from 69 plus 1 from 26).
2. **Wait for the draw.** At a scheduled time, the lottery operator draws numbers using certified random methods — physical ball machines or audited random number generators.
3. **Check your numbers.** If your numbers match the drawn numbers, you win. The more numbers you match, the higher the prize. The jackpot requires matching all numbers exactly.
4. **Prizes are tiered.** Most lotteries pay smaller prizes for partial matches — matching 3, 4, or 5 numbers pays progressively more, even if you miss the jackpot.

### Scratch cards (instant games)

1. **Buy a card.** Each card has a set price and a predetermined set of outcomes. The winning and losing cards are distributed across the entire print run.
2. **Scratch to reveal.** Remove the coating to see your result. The outcome was decided when the card was printed — scratching is just the reveal.
3. **Check the prize table.** Each scratch card game publishes its prize structure, including total number of prizes and odds of winning at each tier. This information is usually printed on the back of the card.

### Syndicates (group play)

A syndicate pools money from multiple players to buy more tickets. More tickets means more chances to match — but any prize is split among all members. Your odds improve linearly (buy 10 tickets, 10x the chance), but your share of any win decreases by the same factor.

## The math

### Jackpot odds

The odds of hitting a lottery jackpot depend on the game format, but they're always extreme.

| Game type | Typical jackpot odds | Context |
|-----------|---------------------|---------|
| Major national draw (6 from 49) | `~1 in 14 million` | About the odds of flipping a coin and getting heads 23 times in a row |
| Major multi-state/national draw (5+1 format) | `1 in 100 million` to `1 in 300 million` | You're more likely to be struck by lightning twice |
| Smaller state/regional draw | `1 in 1 million` to `1 in 10 million` | Better, but still astronomically unlikely |

### RTP (return to player)

<!-- ADAPT: currency -->
| Product | Typical RTP | What it means |
|---------|------------|---------------|
| **Major draw games** | `50–55%` | For every `$100` in tickets bought across all players, about `$50–$55` is paid back as prizes |
| **Scratch cards** | `60–75%` | Better than draws, but still well below casino games |
| **Add-on multipliers** | `Varies` | Can improve or worsen RTP depending on the specific product |
<!-- /ADAPT -->

For comparison: most casino table games return `95–99%`. Slots return `85–98%`. Lotteries sit well below both.

### What this means for your wallet

<!-- ADAPT: currency -->
For every `$100` you spend on lottery tickets, you'd expect to get back `$50–$65` over time. That's the long-run math — the lottery keeps `35–50%` of all ticket revenue. Some of that funds prize pools. Some funds the operator. Some goes to government programs or good causes, depending on your jurisdiction. But from a pure return-on-wager perspective, lottery has the widest house edge of any mainstream gambling product.
<!-- /ADAPT -->

### Scratch card math

Scratch cards vary more than draw games. Each game publishes its overall odds and prize distribution. The RTP ranges from `60%` to `75%` depending on the game and price point. Higher-priced cards often (but not always) have slightly better RTPs. The odds of winning *something* versus winning *enough to cover the ticket price* are very different numbers — check both.

**Compared to other games:** See [Odds at a Glance](odds-at-a-glance.md).

## Key terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Draw** | The event where winning numbers are randomly selected. Can be physical (ball machines) or digital (certified RNG). See [glossary](../brand-book/glossary.md#draw). |
| **Jackpot** | The top prize, usually requiring all numbers to match. Often rolls over if not won, growing the prize pool. |
| **Rollover** | When no one wins the jackpot, the prize money carries forward to the next draw, increasing the jackpot. |
| **Syndicate** | A group of players who pool money to buy multiple tickets and split any winnings proportionally. |
| **Scratch card** (instant game) | A ticket with a predetermined outcome revealed by scratching off a coating. Results are set at printing, not at purchase. |
| **Quick pick** (lucky dip) | A ticket where numbers are randomly selected by the machine rather than chosen by the player. |
| **Annuity vs. lump sum** | Two ways to receive a large jackpot: annuity pays out over 20–30 years; lump sum is a single reduced payment. The lump sum is typically 40–60% of the advertised jackpot. |
| **Odds of winning** | The probability of matching enough numbers to win a prize. Published for every lottery product — but the headline odds are usually for *any* prize, not the jackpot. |
| **Print run** | The total number of scratch cards produced for a specific game. Prize distribution is spread across the entire run. |

## Tips for informed play

1. **Every number combination has identical odds.** Birthdays, "lucky" numbers, patterns, and quick picks all have exactly the same probability of being drawn. The balls don't know which numbers you picked or why.
2. **Syndicates improve your odds linearly — and split winnings the same way.** Buy 10 tickets as a group, you have 10 times the chance. Win a prize, it's split 10 ways. The math is straightforward — syndicates don't create value, they redistribute it.
3. **Check scratch card odds before you buy.** The back of the card (or the lottery website) shows the overall odds and prize breakdown. Cards from the same game at the same price can have very different odds depending on how many top prizes remain.
4. **Budget for lottery like entertainment, not investment.** The RTP is `50–65%`. Over time, you'll get back about half to two-thirds of what you spend. Decide what you're comfortable spending for the entertainment value and stick to it.
5. **"Systems" don't change the math.** Software, books, and services that claim to improve your lottery odds are selling hope, not probability. No analysis of past draws can predict future random draws. The draws are independent events.

## Quiz questions

### Question 1

**Stem:** A major national lottery jackpot has odds of 1 in 292 million. You've been playing the same numbers for 10 years without winning. What are your odds of winning on your next ticket?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| A | Better than when you started — you've "paid your dues" |
| B | Worse — the odds are against long-term players |
| C | Exactly 1 in 292 million — the same as everyone else's ticket |
| D | It depends on how many other people play those numbers |

**Correct:** C

**Explanation:** Every draw is an independent random event. The lottery machine has no memory of your previous tickets. Your odds on the next draw are exactly the same as someone buying their first ticket ever: 1 in 292 million. The belief that persistence earns a reward is the gambler's fallacy — it applies to roulette, slots, and lotteries equally. Past draws have zero influence on future draws.

**Source:** Basic probability theory — independence of random events.

### Question 2

**Stem:** Your friend says they only pick birthdays (numbers 1–31) because "those numbers come up all the time." What's the problem with this strategy?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| A | Numbers above 31 come up more often |
| B | Birthday numbers have worse odds of being drawn |
| C | All numbers have equal odds, but birthday numbers are more popular — so if you win, you're more likely to split the prize |
| D | The lottery knows which numbers are popular and avoids them |

**Correct:** C

**Explanation:** Every number has an identical chance of being drawn. But numbers 1–31 are disproportionately popular because people pick birthdays. This doesn't affect your odds of *winning* — but it does affect your odds of winning *alone*. If a jackpot combination is all low numbers, more tickets are likely to match, meaning the prize gets split more ways. Choosing less popular numbers doesn't improve your odds of winning, but it can improve your odds of not sharing.

**Source:** Lottery draw analysis — all numbers have equal probability; player selection patterns are well documented.

### Question 3

<!-- ADAPT: currency -->
**Stem:** A lottery game has an RTP of 55%. You buy $20 worth of tickets every week for a year ($1,040 total). How much would you expect to get back over that period?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| A | About `$1,040` — it evens out over a year |
| B | About `$572` — 55% of what you spent |
| C | About `$900` — the RTP improves over time |
| D | It's impossible to estimate — lottery is pure luck |

**Correct:** B

**Explanation:** RTP is a long-run average. At 55% RTP, for every dollar wagered, the expected return is 55 cents. Over $1,040 in total wagers, the expected return is about $572 — meaning you'd expect to lose roughly $468 over the year. Individual results will vary (you might win nothing or hit a significant prize), but the mathematical expectation is clear. This is why lottery is best budgeted as entertainment spending, not as an investment or savings strategy.

**Source:** RTP calculation: $1,040 × 0.55 = $572.
