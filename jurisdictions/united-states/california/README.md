# California (Tribal Compacts) -- Compliance Module

> **Operator note**: California's tribal gaming regulatory framework is unlike any other US jurisdiction. There is no single set of rules -- each of the 67 ratified tribal-state compacts contains its own responsible gambling provisions, and each of the 60+ tribal gaming commissions sets its own policies. This module documents the **common provisions** found across most compacts and the state-level framework. Operators **must** verify specific requirements against the compact governing their property and the rules of the relevant tribal gaming commission.
>
> **Verticals covered**: Land-based casino only
> *(California does not permit legal online casino gaming or sports betting. Propositions 26 and 27, which would have legalized sports betting, were rejected by voters in November 2022. Tribal leaders have indicated no plans to pursue a 2026 ballot measure; 2028 is the next realistic window.)*

> **Last verified**: 2026-03-22

## Regulatory authority

### Three-layer regulatory structure

California tribal gaming operates under a **hybrid regulatory model** that is unique among US states. Three layers of authority govern tribal casinos:

| Layer | Authority | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Federal** | National Indian Gaming Commission (NIGC) | Oversees Class II gaming; approves tribal gaming ordinances; enforcement authority under IGRA |
| **State** | California Gambling Control Commission (CGCC) + Bureau of Gambling Control (BGC, within CA DOJ) | Suitability determinations for key employees and suppliers; compact administration; SDF/RSTF fund management; casino monitoring |
| **Tribal** | Individual Tribal Gaming Commissions (~60+) | Primary on-site regulation; gaming ordinance enforcement; self-exclusion programs; employee licensing; day-to-day compliance |

### California Gambling Control Commission (CGCC)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Website** | [cgcc.ca.gov](https://www.cgcc.ca.gov/) |
| **Role** | State regulatory body for tribal gaming under compacts |
| **Key functions** | Suitability recommendations; SDF/RSTF fund administration; compliance auditing; compact monitoring |
| **Established** | 1997 (Gambling Control Act, Bus. & Prof. Code s. 19800 et seq.) |

### Bureau of Gambling Control (BGC)

| **Parent** | California Department of Justice (Office of the Attorney General) |
| **Role** | Investigative and enforcement arm |
| **Key functions** | Background investigations of key employees and suppliers; casino inspections; enforcement actions |
| **Field operations** | CES-North: 35 tribal casinos; CES-South: 25 tribal casinos |

### National Indian Gaming Commission (NIGC)

| **Role** | Federal oversight of tribal gaming under IGRA |
| **California office** | Sacramento regional staff |

### Key legislation and compacts

### Compact generations

There are roughly three generations of compacts, each with increasingly detailed responsible gambling provisions:

| Generation | Period | Key characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **1999 Compacts** | 1999 (58 tribes signed with Gov. Davis) | Basic framework; fewer explicit RG provisions |
| **2004-2006 Compacts** | 2004-2006 | Expanded gaming device limits; enhanced revenue sharing; moderate RG provisions |
| **Post-2011 Compacts** | 2011 onwards | Strongest responsible gambling provisions; advertising restrictions; self-exclusion requirements; responsible gambling messaging mandates |

**Important**: Compacts typically last 20 years and are renegotiated upon expiration. The specific terms vary -- operators must review the compact governing their property.

## Legal requirements

| Requirement | Value |
|---|---|
| **Minimum gambling age** | 18+ (casinos without alcohol) or 21+ (casinos with alcohol license) |
| **Legal framework** | Tribal-State Compacts under IGRA; Class III gaming |
| **Online gambling** | **Not legal** -- no online casino or sports betting |
| **Sports betting** | **Not legal** -- Propositions 26 and 27 rejected by voters (Nov 2022) |

### Permitted products

| Product | Legal status | Regulator | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Slot machines (Class III) | Legal | Tribal Gaming Commission / CGCC | Authorized under compacts; number of machines varies by compact |
| Table games (card games) | Legal | Tribal Gaming Commission / CGCC | Banked and percentage card games including blackjack, baccarat, poker |
| Bingo (Class II) | Legal | Tribal Gaming Commission / NIGC | Regulated under IGRA Class II; does not require a compact |
| Lottery games | Legal (tribal) | Tribal Gaming Commission / CGCC | Authorized under compacts |
| Sports betting | **Not legal** | -- | No authorization in California |
| Online casino | **Not legal** | -- | No authorization in California |
| Cardrooms (non-tribal) | Legal | CGCC / BGC | ~65 licensed cardrooms; separate from tribal system |
| Horse racing (pari-mutuel) | Legal | California Horse Racing Board | Separate regulatory body |

### Gaming restrictions

California gambling law has several unique characteristics:

- **No dice or ball games were originally permitted** -- traditional craps and roulette were historically prohibited, though newer and amended compacts have authorized card-based versions and, in some cases, standard craps and roulette
- **No complimentary alcoholic beverages** -- most compacts prohibit tribes from serving free alcohol to gaming patrons
- Tribal casinos operate **more than 70,000 slot machines** and thousands of table games statewide

### Economic scale

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Tribes with ratified compacts | 67 | CGCC (Jan 2025) |
| Secretarial Procedures in effect | 10 additional tribes | CGCC (Jan 2025) |
| Tribes operating casinos | 63 | CGCC / CNIGA |
| Total casinos | 66 | CGCC / CNIGA |
| Estimated annual gross gaming revenue | ~$8.5 billion | AGA (2025) |

### Display rules

Display requirements are **compact-dependent**. Post-2011 compacts typically require:
- Helpline number displayed in advertising and marketing materials
- Responsible gambling information available at the casino
- Referral information for problem gambling services

Because California has no single state-mandated display format, operators should:
1. Check their specific compact for helpline display requirements
2. Display **1-800-GAMBLER** as the primary number (widely recognized, used by CALPG in-state)
3. Include the CALPG website (calpg.org) where space permits
4. Add **1-800-MY-RESET** as the updated national branded number

## Messaging requirements

### Fragmented messaging landscape

California has **no single set of messaging requirements**. Requirements depend on:
1. Which compact governs the property
2. Which generation of compact (1999, 2004-2006, post-2011)
3. The tribal gaming commission's own ordinances

### Messaging regime summary

| Context | Obligation type | What's required | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Casino signage (all compacts)** | Obligation-based | Problem gambling information and helpline available to patrons | Individual compacts |
| **Advertising (post-2011 compacts)** | Obligation-based | Responsible gambling messages and toll-free helpline in advertising materials | Post-2011 compacts |
| **Advertising (pre-2011 compacts)** | Minimal / none | Earlier compacts have few or no explicit advertising messaging requirements | Individual compacts |
| **Patron communications** | Obligation-based | Responsible gambling information in patron communications (newer compacts) | Post-2011 compacts |

### Verbatim required statements

**None** -- California compacts do not prescribe verbatim responsible gambling text. This is obligation-based: operators must communicate responsible gambling information and helpline access, but the exact wording is at the operator's and tribal gaming commission's discretion.

### On-brand integration

Because there are no mandated verbatim statements, operators have **full flexibility** to use {{PROGRAM_NAME}} messaging:

**Generic operator approach:**
> "If gambling is a problem, call 1-800-GAMBLER."

**{{PROGRAM_NAME}} on-brand approach:**
> **Play on your terms.** Set your limits. Know the odds. And if you ever want to talk, free confidential support is available 24/7.
> **1-800-GAMBLER** | **calpg.org**

**Rules for on-brand integration:**
1. Include the helpline number prominently (compact requirement for post-2011 compacts)
2. Use full {{PROGRAM_NAME}} voice -- no verbatim constraints
3. Give all messaging visual dignity -- do not shrink to minimum size
4. Follow with a helpful action whenever possible
5. Verify against your specific compact's requirements

## Advertising restrictions

### Compact-dependent framework

Advertising restrictions in California are **not uniform** -- they depend on the compact governing the property. Post-2011 compacts contain the most detailed advertising provisions.

### Common provisions (post-2011 compacts)

| Rule | Requirement | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Responsible gambling messaging** | Advertising materials must contain responsible gambling messages | Post-2011 compacts |
| **Helpline inclusion** | Toll-free problem gambling helpline number in advertising | Post-2011 compacts |
| **No targeting minors** | Advertising must not appeal to children or adolescents | Compact provisions |
| **No wealth promises** | Advertising must not offer gambling as a means of becoming wealthy | Compact provisions |
| **Truthfulness** | Advertising that is presumptively deceptive is prohibited | Compact provisions |

### FTC and general law

| Rule | Requirement | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Truth-in-advertising** | Standard FTC rules apply to all gambling advertising | Federal (FTC Act) |
| **No false claims** | No misleading claims about odds, winnings, or guaranteed outcomes | FTC / state consumer protection |

### Channel-specific considerations

| Channel | Key considerations |
|---|---|
| **Broadcast (TV/radio)** | FCC and FTC general advertising rules apply; no California-specific broadcast gambling ad ban |
| **Digital / social** | FTC guidelines; platform-specific policies (Meta, Google restrict gambling ads) |
| **Print** | General consumer protection law |
| **Direct marketing** | Self-excluded patrons must be removed from marketing (where self-exclusion is in effect) |
| **In-venue** | Tribal gaming commission rules; compact provisions |

### Important caveat

Pre-2011 compacts have **few or no explicit advertising restrictions**. Operators under older compacts should still follow industry best practices (AGA Responsible Gaming Code of Conduct) and verify with their tribal gaming commission.

## Self-exclusion

### Property-by-property model

California has **no centralized statewide self-exclusion program** for tribal casinos. Each tribal gaming commission operates its own program independently.

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Model** | Property-by-property (individual tribal gaming commissions) |
| **Centralized program** | **None** -- no statewide or multi-tribal registry |
| **Number of programs** | 60+ (one per tribal gaming commission) |
| **Duration options** | Vary by property |
| **Enrollment method** | Varies -- in-person, mail-in, or at the tribal gaming commission (not inside the casino) |
| **Scope** | Typically covers the specific tribal casino only |

### State-level self-exclusion (non-tribal)

The California Department of Justice operates a [Self-Exclusion Program](https://oag.ca.gov/gambling/exclusion_self) for **state-licensed cardrooms** (non-tribal). This program does **not** apply to tribal casinos.

### What newer compacts require

Post-2011 compacts typically require tribal gaming operations to allow persons to enroll in a self-exclusion program. Key provisions include:

- Halting promotional mailings
- Revoking privileges for casino services
- Denying or restraining the person's ability to use the facility for check-cashing services
- Denying credit

### {{PROGRAM_NAME}} language mapping

| Context | {{PROGRAM_NAME}} term | Official term | When to use official term |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 (casual) | "Take a break from [casino name]" | "Self-exclusion" | Never in Tier 1 |
| Tier 2 (formal) | "Self-exclusion" | Per tribal gaming commission | Legal documents, formal enrollment |
| Website/app | "Need a break from playing here?" | "Self-exclusion program" | On the self-exclusion information page |
| Print/signage | "Need a break from gambling?" | "Self-exclusion" | Venue self-exclusion information areas |

### Key difference from other states

Unlike New Jersey (statewide), Massachusetts (statewide), or the UK (GAMSTOP national scheme), California's self-exclusion is **entirely fragmented**. A player who self-excludes at one tribal casino is **not** excluded from any other tribal casino. {{PROGRAM_NAME}} content should:

- Clarify that exclusion applies to this property only
- Provide information on how to self-exclude at other properties if the player requests it
- Note that the state DOJ program covers cardrooms but not tribal casinos

## AML/KYC

### FinCEN/BSA requirements

Tribal casinos are classified as **financial institutions** under the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) and must comply with FinCEN regulations, just like commercial casinos:

| Requirement | Threshold | Player-facing impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Currency Transaction Report (CTR)** | $10,000+ (single or aggregate in 24 hours) | Player must provide ID; transaction reported to FinCEN |
| **Suspicious Activity Report (SAR)** | Any amount | No direct player notification -- internal reporting |
| **Player identification** | $10,000+ cash transactions | Government-issued photo ID required |
| **Record keeping** | $3,000+ (cash purchases of chips, TITO) | Operator must record and retain transaction details |

### Tribal compliance

Each tribal gaming commission is responsible for ensuring its casino complies with BSA/AML requirements. The NIGC also has oversight authority for AML compliance at tribal gaming operations.

### Player-facing messaging

**Bare compliance:**
> "Federal law requires us to verify your identity for transactions of $10,000 or more."

**On-brand:**
> "Quick ID check -- it keeps your account secure and is required by federal law. If we ask about a large transaction, it's a standard process that applies to everyone at every casino in the US."
