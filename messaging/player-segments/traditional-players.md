# Traditional Players

Messaging for players whose primary gambling is lottery, bingo, horse racing, or land-based casino. Many are long-time players with decades of experience across these forms. They gamble at venues, at retailers, and at the track as often as (or more than) they gamble online. Their gambling is frequently social — syndicates, race days, bingo nights — and the communal experience is often the point.

> **Operator note**: This segment skews 50+ but is not defined by age. A 35-year-old who plays bingo every Thursday and buys scratch cards at the corner shop belongs here too. The defining trait is the *gambling form*, not the birth year. Do not patronize this audience. Many of these players have been gambling longer than most responsible gambling programs have existed. They are experienced, not naive. Treat them as the knowledgeable players they are. See [Voice and Tone](../../brand-book/04-voice-and-tone.md) for the full framework.

## Segment profile

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Who** | Players whose primary gambling involves lottery, bingo, horse racing, or land-based casino |
| **Demographics** | Skews 50+ but includes all ages. Mix of urban and regional. Often long-tenured players with established routines. |
| **Motivations** | Social activity, routine and ritual, entertainment, the dream (lottery), the sport (racing), the atmosphere (bingo halls, casino floors) |
| **Relationship to gambling** | Experienced and comfortable. Gambling is a familiar part of their leisure life, not a novelty. Many have well-established habits — weekly lottery tickets, regular bingo nights, race day traditions. |
| **Products** | Lottery (draw games, scratch cards), bingo (venue and online), horse racing (win/place, each-way, accumulators), casino floor (slots, table games), occasionally online versions of these |
| **Media habits** | Television, print newspapers, direct mail, radio, venue-based media (posters, table tents, rack cards). Digital is secondary — some use email, some browse news sites, some are on Facebook. Few are on Instagram or TikTok. |
| **Social context** | High. Bingo nights with friends, lottery syndicates at work or in the neighborhood, race day as a group outing, casino trips with a partner or group. Gambling is often embedded in social life. |
| **Self-perception** | "I've been doing this for years — I know how it works." (They know a lot. Some of what they know is wrong, but they've earned the right to be spoken to as equals.) |

## Key insight

> **This segment doesn't need to be introduced to gambling. They need their existing knowledge sharpened — gently, respectfully, and without any suggestion that they've been doing it wrong for 30 years.**

Traditional players have deep familiarity with their chosen games. They understand the rhythms, the rituals, the social codes. What they may not have encountered is a clear, non-judgmental explanation of the math behind the games they already love. Lottery players know the odds are long but may not know *how* long. Bingo players understand the social contract of a session but may not think about cumulative spend across months of play. Racing enthusiasts read the form guide but may overestimate its predictive power.

The opportunity: meet them where they already are — at the venue, in the syndicate group, at the newsagent counter — with information that adds to what they know rather than correcting what they believe. Playbook isn't here to tell a 60-year-old bingo player how gambling works. It's here to hand them a useful fact they might not have seen before.

## Message priorities

In order of importance for this segment:

1. **Cumulative spend awareness** — The individual purchase is small (a lottery ticket, a book of bingo cards, a few each-way bets). The cumulative cost over weeks and months is where the real number lives. Help them see the total, not to alarm them, but because knowing the number is useful.
2. **How randomness actually works** — Lucky numbers, "due" draws, hot streaks on the casino floor. These myths are deeply embedded and socially reinforced. Correct with facts, not finger-wagging.
3. **Form guides and systems are not guarantees** — Racing form, lottery number-picking systems, and roulette strategies all feel like knowledge-based edges. Separate genuine information (form is real data) from the false conclusion (form doesn't guarantee outcomes).
4. **Session and spend tracking for venue play** — Deposit limits are designed for online accounts. This segment needs tools for tracking time and money at the venue, at the retailer, and across repeated low-value purchases.
5. **Social reinforcement** — Gambling is communal for this segment. Messages that work within the group ("share this with your syndicate") are stronger than messages aimed at the individual in isolation.

## Tone guidance

**Voice**: Playbook voice (Confident / Informative → Warm / Direct)

Traditional players respond to warmth, directness, and genuine knowledge. They can spot condescension immediately, and they will not tolerate it. The voice treats them as experienced players who are being offered a useful piece of information — not a lesson, not a warning, not a correction.

| Register | When to use with this segment |
|---|---|
| **Confident / Informative** | Odds education, lottery math, racing probability, how-it-works explainers |
| **Warm / Direct** | Spend tracking, session awareness, venue-based check-ins, tool promotion |
| **Playful / Witty** | Myth-busting (light touch), bingo and racing humor, syndicate content |
| **Celebratory** | Milestone moments, tool setup, quiz completions |

### Critical tone note

**Not condescending about age or experience.** Never imply that long-time players need "updating" or that their experience is a liability. "You've been playing for years — but did you know this?" is dangerously close to condescension. "Here's a number most people haven't seen" treats them as part of a larger group discovering something new together.

## Sample messages

### Venue visit

> "Heading to the casino? Set your budget before you walk through the door. Knowing your number makes the night better, not worse."

> "Time check: you've been on the floor for {{DURATION}}. No rush — just a heads-up so you can decide what's next."

> "The slots at the venue run the same math as the ones online. Same RTP, same house edge, same independence between spins. The carpet's just nicer."

### Lottery purchase

> "A lottery ticket is a couple of dollars for a dream. That's a fair deal — as long as you know what you're buying. The odds of a 6/49 jackpot? 1 in 13,983,816. Every single draw."

> "Your lucky numbers have a 1-in-14-million chance. So do the numbers you'd never pick. The draw doesn't play favorites."

> "Buying a ticket every week? That's your call. Knowing the weekly, monthly, and yearly total? That's information worth having."

> "Scratch cards: the odds of the top prize are printed on the back. Most people never check. Take a look."

### Bingo session

> "Bingo is one of the most social forms of gambling out there. The numbers are random, but the company isn't. Set a budget for the session and enjoy the room."

> "Every bingo card has the same chance of winning. Buying more cards increases your chances per game, but it also increases the cost of the session. Know both numbers."

> "Thursday night bingo. Same hall, same friends, same fun. Quick thought: what does a typical month of sessions cost you? Not a warning — just a number worth knowing."

### Horse racing

> "The form guide is real data. It tells you what happened. It doesn't tell you what will happen. Horses don't read their own stats."

> "A 10/1 shot means the bookmaker thinks it has roughly a 9% chance. The 'roughly' is where the bookmaker's margin lives."

> "Each-way betting: you're placing two bets, not one. Win and place. Make sure the total stake is what you intended to spend."

> "Race day with friends is one of the best days out in gambling. Set your budget for the card before the first race. It's easier to enjoy the last race when you've already decided what the day costs."

### Tool promotion

> "Spend tracker: see what your lottery tickets, bingo sessions, and race days actually cost you per month. No judgment — just the number."

> "Session reminders work at the venue too. Set a time limit on your phone before you walk in. It's a two-second decision that gives you information later."

> "Most tools are built for online accounts. If you play at venues, a simple budget note on your phone works just as well. Write down the number before you go. Check it when you leave."

## Channel emphasis

| Channel | Priority | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Venue-based (posters, rack cards, table tents, washroom ads)** | Primary | This is where these players are. Physical media at the point of play is the highest-impact placement. |
| **Television** | Primary | High reach with this demographic. PSA spots, pre-race advertising, bingo hall screen content. |
| **Print (newspapers, racing programs, bingo schedules)** | Primary | Trusted medium for this audience. Race-day programs, newspaper inserts, bingo hall printed materials. |
| **Direct mail** | Primary | Effective for loyalty-program players and venue regulars. Physical mail is read; email often isn't. |
| **Radio** | Secondary | Reaches commuters and habitual listeners. Good for lottery and racing awareness. |
| **Email** | Secondary | Works for players who have opted in. Keep frequency low and content substantive. |
| **Facebook** | Secondary | The one social platform with strong adoption in this segment. Use for event-based content (race previews, bingo specials). |
| **Website / blog** | Secondary | For players who seek out information. Long-form content for those who want to go deeper. |
| **Instagram / TikTok / Twitter/X** | Low | Most of this segment is not here. Don't build campaigns around platforms they don't use. |
| **Push notifications** | Low | Relevant only for players who use a mobile app, which is a subset of this segment. |

### Channel-native content tips

- **Venue posters and rack cards**: One idea per piece. Large type. A single fact and a single action. "The odds of a 6/49 jackpot: 1 in 13,983,816. Every draw. Know more at {{URL}}."
- **TV spots**: 15–30 seconds. Warm tone, real scenarios (at the bingo hall, at the shop counter, at the track). End with one clear takeaway.
- **Print inserts**: Can carry more information than a poster. Use for the spend-tracker concept, odds breakdowns, or myth-busting one-pagers.
- **Direct mail**: Personalized where possible. "Here's your play summary for the quarter" works well for venue loyalty programs.
- **Facebook**: Shareable facts, not awareness campaigns. "Did you know?" posts that feel like a useful discovery, not an ad.

## What to avoid

| Don't | Why | Do instead |
|---|---|---|
| Frame older players as vulnerable or confused | This is the fastest way to lose this audience. They have decades of experience and they know it. | Treat them as experienced players being offered a useful new fact. "Most people haven't seen this number" — not "You need to know this." |
| Use digital-first language ("swipe up," "tap here," "check your app") | Many in this segment don't primarily use apps. Digital-only framing excludes them. | "Check the number." "Ask at the counter." "See the back of the card." Meet them in their actual environment. |
| Assume they don't understand gambling | They understand it deeply — in practice, if not always in math. Implying ignorance is insulting. | Add to what they know. "Here's a number that might surprise you" respects existing knowledge. |
| Ignore the social dimension | This segment gambles communally. Messaging aimed only at solitary individuals misses the context. | "Share this with your syndicate." "Bring this to bingo night." Use communal framing. |
| Lecture about spend on small purchases | "You spent $5 on a lottery ticket" sounds absurd to someone who spends $5 on coffee. The individual purchase isn't the issue. | Focus on the cumulative number. "Your weekly ticket adds up to $260 a year. Not a problem — just a number worth knowing." |
| Use youth-oriented design or language | Memes, slang, emoji-heavy copy, and trendy aesthetics signal "this isn't for you." | Clean, clear, warm design. Readable type sizes. Straightforward language. |
| Dismiss their rituals as irrational | Lucky numbers, favorite machines, pre-race routines — these are part of the experience, not evidence of confusion. | Acknowledge the ritual, then add the math. "Your lucky number is as good as any other — every number has the same odds. Play the one that makes you smile." |
| Compare them unfavorably to younger players | "Unlike digital players, this segment needs..." creates an unwanted hierarchy. | Every segment has knowledge gaps. Address this segment on its own terms. |
